# Could the commercial arm import for the government? The import case is stronger than export

*Strategic evaluation. Synthesises the project's import-parity data, PSU-failure history,
and comparative models with verified 2025-26 import facts. July 2026.*

## The counter-intuitive finding: importing suits a state arm *better* than exporting

Every prior report in this project concluded that a state *export* arm is a hard business —
thin margins, the reliability trap, SEBI's hedging ban, the STC→PEC→NCEL serial-failure
record. Flip to imports and three of those four objections weaken or invert. **An import arm
is the better-fitting mandate**, for four asymmetric reasons — but it carries one governance
risk that is worse than export, and the history is a genuine warning.

## Why imports fit a state arm where exports don't

### 1. Imports ARE hedgeable — the single decisive asymmetry

The domestic grain book cannot be hedged: SEBI suspended paddy/wheat/chana/mustard/soy
futures (Dec 2021, extended through 2026) — the state banned its own tools. But an **import**
book is priced off *international* exchanges that are fully open to Indian buyers:

- **Bursa Malaysia CPO futures** (palm — India's largest edible-oil import)
- **CBOT soybean-oil futures** and the CBOT–BMD spread
- **CME's new South Asia Crude Palm Oil futures** (launched 2 Mar 2026, priced CFR West
  Coast India — i.e., the *landed cost* an Indian importer actually faces)

A government import arm buying 6.7 MT of pulses and ~₹19.5 bn of edible oil a year could
**lock its landed cost on liquid international markets** — turning the naked directional
long that sank the 2006-11 pulses imports into a hedged, spread-managed book. This is the
capability the export/domestic arm structurally lacks. *(Sources: CME South Asia CPO launch,
Mar 2026; Bursa/CBOT spread specs — CME Group.)*

### 2. Monopsony power — the buyer's advantage in a deficit market

On exports India is one seller among many (competing with Thailand/Vietnam/Pakistan on
price). On imports it is one of the *largest buyers on earth* — 6.7 MT pulses (FY25),
2.2 MT yellow peas alone. That is **monopsony leverage**: the Egypt GASC model, where a
single state importer running open tenders extracts better terms than fragmented private
buyers. India's G2G pulse pacts (Mozambique 200k t tur, Myanmar 100k t tur + 250k t urad,
Malawi 50k t tur — ~350k t assured to 2025-26, likely extended five years) already use this
leverage, just without the hedging or the tender transparency.

### 3. Aligned incentives — no regulator-trader conflict on the import side

The fatal flaw of a state *export* arm is that exporting competes with the PDS/buffer
mandate (sell abroad vs feed the poor — the 2022 wheat-ban whiplash). An *import* arm points
the *same direction* as the buffer mandate: import to cool domestic prices, build the pulse/
oil buffer, cover the deficit. Buying cheap abroad to stabilise prices at home is exactly
what the buffer-holder wants — the conflict that dooms the export arm largely dissolves.

### 4. A built-in exit — success is shrinking itself

An export arm wants to grow forever. An import arm's explicit success metric is **import
substitution** — NMEO-Oilseeds and the Pulses Atmanirbharta mission (NAFED/NCCF 100%
tur/urad/masur procurement to 2028-29) aim to make the imports *unnecessary*. The arm is
designed to work itself out of a job as domestic production rises — a self-limiting mandate,
unlike open-ended trading.

## The warning: imports have the *worse* PSU track record

Government importing is not new, and its history is uglier than exporting:

- **The 2006-11 pulses imports**: NAFED/PEC/STC/MMTC lost ₹113.4 cr, Cabinet-reimbursed —
  naked, unhedged, politically-timed buying in a volatile market
  ([psu-agri-ventures-history.md](psu-agri-ventures-history.md)).
- **The 1991-92 palmolein scam**: Kerala Civil Supplies edible-oil import, ₹2.32 cr loss,
  CAG-indicted — the corruption risk when a state body picks import counterparties.
- **Today's G2G pulse pacts run on nomination**, the same mechanism as NCEL's 3-4% export
  execution — opaque counterparty selection, no competitive tender, no published hedge.

The lesson is not "don't import" — it is "don't import the way India imported before."

## The one governance risk that is worse than export

An import monopsony that *also* sets the tariff and holds the buffer is the
regulator-trader-buffer conflict again — amplified. On imports the state controls **three
levers at once**: how much to import, the customs duty (tur duty-free vs yellow peas 30% vs
masur 10% — all set by the government), and the buffer release. A single arm holding all
three can move domestic prices arbitrarily and mask losses across the three books. This is
the import-side version of the conflict that killed the trading STEs — and it is *more*
concentrated than on the export side.

## Verdict and design

**Yes — the commercial arm should import for the government, and imports are the better core
mandate than exports.** But only under four guardrails, three of which directly answer the
failure history:

1. **Hedge every position on international exchanges — mandate it, don't allow it.** The
   tool (Bursa/CBOT/CME South Asia CPO) exists precisely where the domestic tool was banned.
   An unhedged state import book is the 2006-11 loss waiting to recur.
2. **Separate the tariff pen from the import desk.** Duty-setting must stay with a distinct
   authority (Finance/DGFT), never the importing arm — or the monopsony rigs its own floor.
3. **Import by transparent open tender (GASC model), not nominated G2G.** Publish the tender,
   the hedge, and the landed cost. G2G pacts can set assured *volumes*; price discovery must
   still be competitive.
4. **Mandate is counter-cyclical + import-substitution, with a sunset.** Import to stabilise
   and to bridge the deficit while NMEO/Pulses-Atmanirbharta close it — measured by imports
   *displaced*, not turnover booked.

This dovetails with the diversification thesis
([product-diversification-strategy.md](product-diversification-strategy.md)): the deficit
crops (pulses, oils) are where pricing power and strategic value live, and the import arm is
the buy-side of the same book — procure domestically *and* import the shortfall, both hedged,
both aimed at the ₹19.5 bn oil + $3.74 bn pulse import bill as the addressable prize. It also
fits the comparative models
([comparative-farm-support-models.md](comparative-farm-support-models.md) when complete):
Egypt's GASC and Indonesia's BULOG are state *importers/logistics* agencies that work
precisely because they buy and stock rather than guarantee open-ended domestic prices — the
import-and-reserve function is where state agencies have their best global track record.

## Caveats

Hedging feasibility assumes RBI permits the arm's overseas-exchange margin flows at scale
(individual corporates hedge abroad today; a sovereign-scale book's forex/margin treatment
needs confirmation — flagged, not verified). Import volumes/duties are 2025-26 vintage and
change by notification. The monopsony-leverage claim assumes the arm does not itself become
the price-rigging problem — guardrail 2 is load-bearing. GASC/BULOG "success" is relative;
both have had procurement-corruption and fiscal episodes documented in the comparative report.
