## 2. The price architecture — and the squeeze it built

Sugarcane runs on two administered prices moving at different speeds:

- **FRP (input floor, statutory)**: ₹355/qtl at 10.25% recovery for 2025-26 — up **29% since 2019**. Mills *must* pay within 14 days (Sugarcane Control Order 1966), with 15% interest and revenue-recovery attachment on default.
- **Sugar MSP (output floor)**: **frozen at ₹31/kg since February 2019** — seven-plus years unchanged.

Result, per ISMA (Feb 2026): production cost ≈ **₹41.6/kg** vs pan-India ex-mill realization of **₹38–39/kg** — mills lose ₹2.6–3.6 on every kg of sugar sold domestically ([ChiniMandi](https://www.chinimandi.com/rising-costs-stagnant-prices-push-cane-arrears-up-15-to-rs-16087-crore/)).

## 3. Where the stress goes: arrears

An obligation to buy does not create the money to pay. When mill margins turn negative,
the statutory guarantee converts price risk into **farmer credit risk**:

- National cane arrears: **₹16,087 cr (mid-Feb 2026), +15% YoY**; a parliamentary panel
  flagged dues rising **32×** over the comparison window ([ChiniMandi](https://www.chinimandi.com/sugarcane-dues-to-farmers-rose-32-times-parliamentary-panel-raises-concern/)).
- State-wise (2025-26): **Karnataka ₹4,956 cr** (highest), Maharashtra ₹4,252 cr in-season
  peak, **UP ₹3,287 cr — up from just ₹24 cr in 2023-24** (a 137× blow-up in two seasons),
  Gujarat ₹1,402 cr, Punjab ₹535 cr, Haryana ₹373 cr.
- The counterpoint: enforcement works where the state pushes. Maharashtra's sugar
  commissioner ran recovery actions against defaulting mills and got FRP payment to
  **99.02%** by June 2026, with only ₹298–396 cr outstanding ([ChiniMandi](https://www.chinimandi.com/maharashtra-sugar-mills-clear-99-of-frp-dues-for-2025-26-season-rs-396-crore-still-outstanding/)).
  UP's 137× deterioration vs Maharashtra's 99% clearance is a governance gap, not an
  economics gap — the same statute, differently enforced.

## 4. The relief valve is a quantity instrument: ethanol

The Centre's actual tool for keeping the FRP system solvent is not the frozen sugar MSP —
it is the **ethanol offtake channel**, adjusted season by season:

- ESY 2025-26: juice/B-heavy/C-heavy diversion allowed **without restriction**
  ([ChiniMandi](https://www.chinimandi.com/govt-allows-sugar-mills-distilleries-to-produce-ethanol-from-juice-sugar-syrup-bhm-chm-in-esy-2025-26-without-restriction/));
  sugar-route allocation ≈ **289 crore litres (~28% share, ~34 LMT sugar diverted)** against
  NFCSF's ask of 50 LMT — grain (maize ~45% of allocations) took the larger share.
- Mills responded structurally: **56.1% of cane crushed via the B-heavy route** (51.4% LY).
- Industry asks are all price/quantity, not statute: raise sugar MSP to ₹41/kg, raise
  juice/BHM ethanol prices, approve more diversion
  ([NFCSF via ChiniMandi](https://www.chinimandi.com/nfcsf-urges-govt-to-raise-sugar-msp-to-rs-41-kg-increase-ethanol-prices-approve-additional-sugar-diversion-for-ethanol-production/)).

Note the symmetry with the grain side ([main report §4](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md)): for
rice the state owns the stock and *mandates* distillery offtake (40% FCI-grain tender
clause); for cane the mills own the stock and the state *rations* their access to the
same ethanol demand. In both cases the binding policy lever is the administered
**quantity** allocated to the offtake channel — the price announcements are downstream.

## 5. Lessons for the MSV design

1. **An obligation-to-buy without buyer viability manufactures arrears, not income.**
   The ₹16,087 cr is exactly the "credit-risk conversion" our main report warned about —
   the same failure the sugarcane FRP model exhibits is what an MSV imposed on private
   processors (without margins) would reproduce.
2. **Freezing the output price while raising the input floor is the design error.** FRP
   +29% with sugar MSP frozen 7 years is a policy-made margin squeeze. An MSV that fixes
   a procurement floor must let the disposal price (OMSS reserve, ethanol rate,
   PDS issue price) move with it — or budget the gap openly.
3. **Enforcement is a state variable.** Maharashtra 99% vs UP's 137× arrears blow-up under
   the same statute: any MSV act inherits this — a legal guarantee is only as good as the
   commissioner enforcing it.
4. **Quantity allocation is the real throttle.** The Centre manages the whole system by
   deciding *how much* sugar may become ethanol — proof that Indian food-policy already
   operates volume instruments at scale; MSV would formalize on the procurement side what
   ethanol allocation already does on the disposal side.
