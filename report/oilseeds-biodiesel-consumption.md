# Oilseeds, biodiesel and the edible-oil demand map: does procurement ease the oil deficit?

*Three linked questions on India's edible-oil economy — whether MSP procurement of
groundnut and mustard materially eases cooking-oil demand (the arithmetic), why biodiesel
underperformed and where the one food-safe feedstock chain actually sits (the rice
byproduct), and what consumption patterns say about a preference-led vs price-led import
substitution. Cross-references — not repeating —
[domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md](domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md) and
[product-diversification-strategy.md](product-diversification-strategy.md), which cover the
oilseed import-substitution thesis at strategy level. Every figure carries a source and a
verified / reported / unverified label. July 2026.*

Companion data: [`data/edible_oil_consumption.csv`](../data/edible_oil_consumption.csv),
[`data/rice_byproduct_massbalance.csv`](../data/rice_byproduct_massbalance.csv),
[`data/rice_biorefinery_economics.csv`](../data/rice_biorefinery_economics.csv).

**Four questions:** (Q1) does groundnut+mustard MSP procurement ease cooking-oil demand —
the arithmetic; (Q2) why biodiesel underperformed and the food-safe rice-byproduct feedstock
chain it skipped; (Q3) consumption/preference patterns; (Q4) the economic case for processing
the rice byproduct instead of selling it raw.

## Q1 — Would MSP procurement of groundnut + mustard actually ease cooking-oil demand?

### The gross ceiling (what the crops *could* yield)

| Crop | Production 2024-25 | Oil recovery | Gross oil if 100% crushed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mustard/rapeseed | 115 LMT (verified, SEA/govt) | 35% (mid) | **~40 LMT** |
| Groundnut | 119.4 LMT (verified, record, govt 3rd adv. est.) | 40% (mid) | ~48 LMT gross |

*(Production: SEA / Agriculture Ministry 3rd Advance Estimates 2024-25 — mustard 115.2 LMT,
groundnut record 119.4 LMT, total oilseeds 426.09 LMT. Verified.)*

But the gross figure is **the wrong number**, and this is the crux of the honest arithmetic:

- **Mustard is already ~entirely crushed into oil.** Domestic mustard/rapeseed oil output is
  already **~42 LMT** (USDA MY23/24, verified) — i.e. essentially the whole crop already
  reaches consumers as oil, via private trade. It is India's **largest indigenous edible
  oil**. Procuring 25–30% of the mustard crop and crushing it does **not add a single tonne
  of oil to national supply** — it changes *who* crushes the same seed, not *how much* oil
  exists. Incremental oil from mustard procurement per se ≈ **0**.
- **Groundnut is only ~25–30% crushed for oil.** ~half the crop goes to direct food, seed,
  and export (table/confectionery/HPS grades), so actual groundnut oil is only **~12 LMT**
  (USDA MY23/24, verified), not the 48 LMT gross. Procurement *could* divert some table-grade
  groundnut into crushing — but that trades a high-value food/export use for a lower-value
  oil use, which is value-destroying, not a free win.

**So procurement, as procurement, is a near-zero lever on the oil deficit.** The seed is
already grown and already crushed; moving it through a government warehouse does not create
oil. Anyone claiming "procure mustard → ease the oil shortage" is double-counting oil that
already exists in the market.

### The one real lever: production, which assured procurement can *catalyse*

The only way domestic oilseed policy eases the ~160 LMT import gap is by **growing more
oilseed**, and procurement matters only as the *price-assurance mechanism that induces
acreage* — the enabler, not the lever itself.

- NMEO-Oilseeds targets lifting oilseed output **39 → 69.7 MT by 2030-31** (stated goal, not
  achieved — see [domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md](domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md)
  caveats). *(NMEO PIB, verified as a target.)*
- If that +30 MT of *incremental seed* materialised at a blended ~30% recovery, it yields
  **~9 MT = ~90 LMT of new oil** — which *would* be material against 160 LMT of imports
  (closing ~55% of the gap). But this is a **production** story realised over years, gated on
  yield and acreage, and even at full success leaves India ~40% import-dependent.
- Assured MSP procurement is the trigger that makes farmers plant the extra acreage (mustard
  procurement already runs ~8.8% via NAFED-PSS; groundnut collapsed to ~0.1% — the
  [production-vs-procurement data](../data/production_vs_procurement.csv)). Raising the
  procurement *floor* to 25–30% is defensible as a **supply-response instrument** — but its
  effect on oil supply is indirect, lagged, and capped by how much new land/yield responds.

**Verdict on the deficit lever: marginal-to-zero in-year (the seed is already crushed);
materially useful only as a multi-year production catalyst, and even the full NMEO target
closes only ~half the gap.** Procurement is a farm-income and acreage tool first, an oil-supply
tool a distant second.

### The catch: even the oil it produces does *not* undercut imported palm

Procuring oilseed at MSP helps consumers **only if the resulting oil reaches them cheaper
than imports.** It does not. Crush-cost model (per tonne of oil, illustrative, inputs
labelled):

**Mustard oil ex-mill cost**
- Seed at MSP ₹5,950/qtl = ₹59,500/t; at 35% recovery need 2.86 t seed/t oil → **₹1,70,000/t** seed cost
- Less oilcake credit: ~1.77 t cake/t oil × ~₹25/kg → **−₹44,000/t**
- Plus crush/refine ~₹10,000/t
- **Net ≈ ₹1,36,000/t = ~₹136/kg ex-mill** (before brand/retail margin → ~₹150–165/kg retail,
  consistent with observed mustard-oil wholesale **₹137–164/kg**, 2025-26, reported — Tridge/
  commodityonline)

**Imported palm (the price benchmark)**
- CPO CIF ~$1,000/t (mid-2025, reported — IndexBox/Fibre2Fashion) ≈ ₹85,000/t
- Landed refined palmolein retail in India ~**₹100–120/kg** (reported)

**Groundnut oil** is worse on price: MSP ₹7,263/qtl → seed cost ~₹1,82,000/t oil; net after
cake credit ~₹150/kg ex-mill, retail **₹200+/kg** — a structural premium oil that has *never*
competed with palm on price.

**Conclusion — separate the two effects cleanly:**
1. **Farmer-income effect:** procurement at MSP raises oilseed grower incomes and, behind a
   managed tariff, substitutes imports at the margin. **Real and positive.**
2. **Consumer-price effect:** domestic mustard oil (~₹136/kg ex-mill) is **~20–40% dearer
   than imported palm** (~₹100–120/kg). Procurement therefore does **not** cool the price of
   the cheap-oil segment. It shifts consumption toward a *pricier, preferred* oil — a
   welfare gain in preference terms, a **loss** in cost-of-living terms for price-sensitive
   buyers.

Mustard and groundnut are **premium, preference oils, not palm substitutes on price.** This
distinction is the hinge of the whole synthesis (Q3).

## Q2 — Biodiesel: the plan that underperformed, and the food-safe feedstock chain India skipped

### The record: a 20% target that delivered <1%

| Milestone | Target | Actual | Label | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| National Biodiesel Mission (2009) | 20% blend by 2017, jatropha on **11.2–13.4 M ha** wasteland | jatropha reached ~0.5 M ha, mostly <3-yr plantings; mission collapsed | verified | Down To Earth / Millennium Post "Jatropha fiasco" |
| National Policy on Biofuels 2018 | **B5 (5%) by 2030** | **0.6% in FY25**; <0.1% in Aug 2021 | verified | Ind-Ra; TERI policy brief |

Contrast with **ethanol**, where E20 was **achieved ahead of schedule** (the repo's
[ethanol_allocation.csv](../data/ethanol_allocation.csv) and
[product-diversification-strategy.md](product-diversification-strategy.md) cover the E20
success — 289 cr L, ~34 LMT sugar diverted, ESY 2025-26).

### Why biodiesel failed where ethanol succeeded

The asymmetry is entirely about **feedstock**:
- **Ethanol has a domestic surplus feedstock** — sugarcane and now surplus/broken grain. India
  is long sugar; diverting it to fuel *relieves* a glut.
- **Biodiesel has no domestic surplus oil.** India *imports 56% of its edible oil.* The
  US/EU/Brazil model — burn domestically-surplus soy/rapeseed/palm oil as biodiesel — is
  **structurally impossible for India**: you cannot burn the oil you are short of and
  importing. This is the **food-vs-fuel constraint in its sharpest form.**
- The escape hatch was supposed to be **non-edible tree-borne oilseeds (jatropha, pongamia)**
  on wasteland — which *failed* on agronomy (poor seed yield, no high-yielding cultivar, land
  never materialised; verified, Down To Earth). Jatropha was a research-gap failure, not a
  policy-will failure.
- The remaining feedstock is **used cooking oil (UCO)** via the **RUCO** programme (FSSAI,
  Aug 2018) — but only **~6% of available UCO is collected**; ~94% untapped (reported, FSSAI/
  industry). Blending stalled at 0.6% because there is simply no feedstock chain.

### The food-safe path India's biodiesel program should have taken: **process the rice byproduct, don't threaten the grain**

This is the analytical core. The rice-for-**ethanol** route burns the *whole grain* (the
food-vs-fuel critique in the repo). But the rice-**milling byproduct** chain — husk + bran —
yields energy, edible oil **and** biodiesel feedstock **without consuming one kilo of food
grain.** Full mass balance in
[`data/rice_byproduct_massbalance.csv`](../data/rice_byproduct_massbalance.csv).

**1. Milling mass balance (verified ratios).** 100 kg paddy → **67 kg rice + ~22 kg husk +
~8 kg bran + ~3 kg loss** (FCI CMR outturn 67%, verified — CAG/FCI norm; husk 20-22% and bran
8-12%, verified — IRRI Rice Knowledge Bank / DCMSME). Applied to scale:

| | National 2024-25 | FCI-procured-linked (832 LMT paddy) |
|---|---|---|
| Paddy | ~2,240 LMT | 832 LMT |
| Rice (food) | ~1,501 LMT | 558 LMT |
| **Husk** | **~493 LMT** | **~183 LMT** |
| **Bran** | **~179 LMT** | **~67 LMT** |

*(National paddy derived from 150.18 MT milled rice 2024-25 ÷ 0.67; FCI+state CMR 832 LMT
paddy KMS 2024-25 per [omss-processing-returns.md](omss-processing-returns.md). Verified/derived.)*

**2. The critical distinction — what does the government actually touch?** FCI procures
**paddy** and hands it to **private CMR millers**, who do the split, return **67% as rice**,
and **keep the husk and bran.** When FCI later sells via **OMSS(D), it sells already-milled
rice** — the husk and bran were separated *upstream at the CMR mill and never entered FCI's
hands.* So:

> Effectively **~100% of government paddy is milled by private CMR millers**, and the entire
> ~183 LMT husk + ~67 LMT bran byproduct stream is **structurally gifted to millers at the
> CMR split** — FCI touches essentially **zero** husk/bran. This *is* the **₹7,650 cr/yr
> byproduct giveaway** quantified in [omss-processing-returns.md](omss-processing-returns.md)
> Finding 3. The state's saleable product is food rice; every downstream biofuel/oil/silica
> rupee in the byproduct chain is already in private hands before FCI can act.

That is the answer to "how much of the grain sold to private actually gets converted into
rice and husk": all of it is converted by private millers, and the non-rice fractions
(husk+bran, ~30% of paddy mass) are retained by them, not by FCI.

**3. Husk valorisation (~22% of paddy, a ~490 LMT non-food stream).** Rice husk is already a
boiler fuel at mills (**calorific 14–16 MJ/kg**, verified — IOPscience). Higher-value routes:
**rice husk ash is 85–97% silica** (verified — IOPscience), a precursor for
precipitated/bio-silica (tyres, batteries, construction); and **biochar** for carbon
sequestration. Zero food-supply risk, entirely non-edible. Even husk-fired **power co-gen**
(the base case) monetises an otherwise-waste stream at mill scale.

**4. The rice-bran-oil extraction gap — India's under-tapped edible source.** India is the
**world's #1 RBO producer (~10–11 LMT, 36.7% global share, 2024, verified — NITI/market
reports)** yet captures only a fraction of its potential:
- National bran ~179 LMT × ~16–18% oil ≈ **~29 LMT theoretical oil**; SEA-processable bran
  only ~5 MMT (50 LMT) → ~8–9 LMT; PIB/NMEO cites RBO potential **1.62 MMT (16.2 LMT)**
  (reported).
- **India actually extracts ~10–11 LMT.** So it captures only **~⅓ of the national
  bran-oil potential.**
- **Why the gap:** bran degrades within *hours* of milling — endogenous lipase drives free
  fatty acids **>5–10% within hours**, rendering unstabilised bran unfit for edible-grade oil
  (verified — PMC review on rice lipases). ~70% of paddy still passes through huller mills or
  goes to feed unstabilised (reported — technical evaluations). **Stabilisation + modern
  solvent extraction *is* the opportunity** — and, per
  [domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md](domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md), the
  **~350 SEA solvent plants / ~30 MT crush capacity already exist**; the binding constraint
  is *bran collection and stabilisation at the mill*, not plant capex.

**5. RBO → biodiesel efficiency, and the correct ordering.** RBO transesterifies to biodiesel
at high yield (**~90–93% FAME/FAEE**, verified — multiple process studies), and its
*residual* streams are the real prize for fuel: **rice bran acid oil, soapstock, and fatty
acid distillate (RBFAD)** — the **non-edible** byproducts of RBO refining — are a genuine
biodiesel feedstock that **does not compete with cooking oil** (verified — RBFAD→biodiesel
93% FAEE, ScienceDirect microreactor study). The **value logic dictates the ordering:**

- Edible RBO displaces edible-oil **imports** at ~**₹100–110/kg** (high value, forex saved).
- Biodiesel displaces diesel at ~**₹90/L ≈ ₹100/kg** (low value).
- Therefore the efficient cascade is **bran → stabilise → extract *edible* RBO first
  (import substitution) → then route only the acid-oil / soapstock / RBFAD residual +
  de-oiled rice bran (DORB, feed/energy) → biodiesel.**
- **Biodiesel must be the residual use, never the primary one.** Burning extractable edible
  bran oil for fuel while importing palm would destroy ~₹10–20/kg of value — economically
  irrational for an oil-deficit country.

**The food-safe biofuel design (contrast with rice-for-ethanol):**

| | Rice → ethanol | Rice **byproduct** chain |
|---|---|---|
| Feedstock | whole milled rice (FOOD) | husk + bran (NON-food, ~30% of paddy) |
| Food-supply risk | **direct** — diverts grain | **none** |
| Edible-oil impact | none | **+ RBO substitutes palm/soy imports** |
| Fuel output | ethanol (petrol blend) | biodiesel from non-edible residual + husk energy |
| State's current posture | sells rice cheap to distillers (₹2,320) | **gives husk+bran away free (₹7,650 cr)** |

The state, instead of selling raw grain or burning whole rice, should **process the byproduct
chain it currently gifts** — stabilise+extract bran oil (edible, import-substituting),
valorise husk (energy/silica), and route only the non-edible residuals to biodiesel. **Food
supply untouched.** Mechanism: price the byproduct into **CMR auctions** (recover the
₹7,650 cr) or mill on own/HAFED account, so the husk+bran value the state currently gives
away funds the stabilisation+extraction chain.

### Where a state agri arm / MSV framework can build a biodiesel feedstock chain *without* competing with the edible-oil deficit

Three food-safe feedstocks only — everything edible is off-limits by the deficit logic:
1. **Non-edible RBO residuals** (acid oil, soapstock, RBFAD) + **DORB** — from the bran chain
   above; the state already controls the paddy that generates the bran.
2. **UCO via RUCO** — scale collection from the ~94% currently untapped; this is the feedstock
   for **SAF (sustainable aviation fuel)**, not ground biodiesel. India notified a **1% SAF
   blend from 2027 → 2% 2028 → 5% 2030** (international flights first; gazette 23 Apr 2026,
   verified — PIB/IOC reports). If India collected just **15% of commercial UCO it could
   make ~1.36 MT SAF**, well above the ~0.8 MT needed for the 5%-by-2030 target (reported —
   SAFA). UCO→SAF is the one high-value, food-safe, demand-backed outlet.
3. **Animal fat/tallow, and CBG** (compressed bio-gas from press-mud/husk/agri-residue under
   SATAT) — non-edible energy streams that sidestep the oil deficit entirely.

The MSV framework's contribution is **feedstock aggregation**: the same collection machinery
that would stabilise bran can aggregate UCO, tallow and residues into a food-safe biodiesel/
SAF feedstock pool — the missing supply chain that left blending at 0.6%.

## Q3 — Consumption patterns: a preference map that fights the price map

Full table: [`data/edible_oil_consumption.csv`](../data/edible_oil_consumption.csv).
Consumption shares (2023-24, reported — industry via Business Standard) applied to ~255 LMT
and cross-checked to SEA import volumes and USDA domestic-output breakdown:

| Oil | ~Share | ~LMT | Import share | Regional preference |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Palm** | 37% | ~94 | ~99% imported | **Invisible** — processed food, HoReCa, cheap blends, vanaspati |
| **Soybean** | 20% | ~51 | ~65% | West (Gujarat/MH/MP/Raj) |
| **Mustard/rapeseed** | 14% | ~36–42 | **~0% (domestic)** | North/East — Bengal/Bihar/UP/Punjab/NE (pungency) |
| **Sunflower** | 13% | ~33 | ~90% imported | South/urban; East zone ~19% |
| **Groundnut** | ~5% | ~13 | ~0% (domestic) | Gujarat/Maharashtra & South |
| **Rice bran** | ~4% | ~10 | ~0% (domestic) | Rising, health-positioned |
| **Cottonseed** | ~5% | ~13 | ~0% (domestic) | Cotton belt; blends/vanaspati |
| **Coconut** | ~2% | ~5 | ~0% (domestic) | Kerala/coastal South |
| **Sesame/others** | ~3% | ~7 | ~0% | Sesame: TN, Bengal, Gujarat (premium) |

**Regional preferences (reported — IJARSCT zonal survey; consistent with HCES cooking-oil
patterns):** East zone mustard ~29% / sunflower ~19%; South sunflower ~44% / groundnut ~29% /
rice bran ~7% / palm ~9%; West soybean ~28% / mustard+sunflower ~25%, with groundnut favoured
in Gujarat-Maharashtra; Kerala coconut. **Palm is the one oil with essentially no regional
"preference" constituency** — it is consumed *because it is cheapest*, hidden inside
namkeen, bakery, restaurant frying, vanaspati and blended loose oil, almost never sold as a
branded consumer cooking oil.

**The preference-vs-price tension (central to Q1):** Indians *prefer* their regional oil
(mustard/groundnut/coconut/sesame) but *consume* palm and soy because they are cheaper. So:
- Import substitution via **mustard/groundnut/rice-bran** aligns with **preference** but
  **not with price** — these are the premium oils (Q1: mustard ₹136/kg ex-mill, groundnut
  ₹150+/kg vs palm ₹100–120/kg).
- The ~94 LMT of **palm** — the true mass/price segment — is the part India **cannot grow**
  (oil palm needs humid tropics; domestic CPO only 3.8 LMT even after the oil-palm mission,
  verified — DDNews/NMEO). The mass-price gap is structurally un-substitutable by the
  preferred domestic oils.

## Synthesis — where oilseed procurement / MSV actually creates value

Tying Q1 + Q2 + Q3 together:

1. **Oilseed MSP procurement does *not* ease the oil market in-year (Q1).** The seed is
   already crushed; procurement adds ~0 oil. It is a **farm-income and acreage instrument**,
   material to the deficit only as a multi-year *production* catalyst behind a managed
   tariff — and even the full NMEO target leaves India ~40% import-dependent.

2. **The oil it does produce is a *premium* oil, not a palm price-competitor (Q1+Q3).**
   Domestic mustard/groundnut cost 20–40%+ more than imported palm. Import substitution here
   is a **preference-led premium play** (branded mustard, kachi-ghani, groundnut, rice-bran
   "heart-healthy") — it raises farmer income and saves forex at the *premium* end, but does
   **not** cool the cheap-oil segment that price-sensitive households actually buy.

3. **The mass/price segment (palm, ~94 LMT) is un-growable (Q3)** — India lacks the agronomy.
   No procurement or MSP policy closes the palm gap; only NMEO-Oil Palm acreage (slow, ~3.8
   LMT CPO today) and hedged imports (the [import arm](import-arm-evaluation.md)) address it.

4. **Biodiesel is a dead end via edible oil, but the rice byproduct is a live, food-safe
   outlet (Q2).** India cannot burn edible oil it imports. The one chain that produces energy
   + edible oil + biodiesel feedstock without touching food grain is the **rice husk+bran**
   the state currently *gives away* (₹7,650 cr/yr). The correct cascade is **bran → stabilise
   → edible RBO (import substitution first) → non-edible acid-oil residual → biodiesel;
   husk → energy/silica; UCO → SAF.** Biodiesel is always the *residual*, never the primary use.

**Recommendation — where the MSV / oilseed procurement creates value, in priority order:**

- **First, capture the byproduct the state already controls.** Price husk+bran into CMR
  auctions or mill on public/HAFED account; fund bran **stabilisation** so the ~⅔ of RBO
  potential now lost to lipase is extracted as *edible* oil — the lowest-capex, highest-return
  import substitution in the system (crush capacity already exists). *This is a bigger, surer
  win than raising oilseed MSP procurement.*
- **Second, run oilseed procurement as an acreage/price-assurance programme, not an oil-supply
  claim.** Extend capped MSV procurement (θ ≈ 0.15–0.30) to mustard/groundnut behind a managed
  tariff, sized to farm-income and the *premium* import bill it displaces — and market the
  output as **branded preference oil** (Bharat mustard/groundnut/RBO), the only segment where
  domestic oil has pricing power. Do **not** pitch it as relief for cheap-oil inflation.
- **Third, build the food-safe biofuel feedstock pool** (RBO residuals + UCO→SAF + tallow +
  CBG) as the MSV framework's biodiesel play — the missing supply chain that left blending at
  0.6% — explicitly ring-fenced from the edible-oil deficit.
- **Leave the palm/price mass segment to acreage expansion + hedged imports** — procurement
  cannot touch it.

The through-line: **oilseed procurement is a preference-and-income lever, not a
price-and-deficit lever; the deficit's real levers are production (slow) and the rice
byproduct the state already owns and throws away (fast).**

## Q4 — The economic case: process the rice byproduct, don't sell it raw

The climax. Data: [`data/rice_biorefinery_economics.csv`](../data/rice_biorefinery_economics.csv).
All figures **derived** from the labelled mass-balance and price inputs above unless marked
otherwise; a ±2 pp swing in bran-oil recovery or ±₹5/kg in parity moves the headline ~15%,
so treat ranges, not point estimates, as the finding.

### 1. Headline — the edible-oil import bill this displaces

| Step | Value |
|---|---|
| National bran (2024-25) | ~179 LMT (derived) |
| Theoretical bran-oil at 16–18% | ~29 LMT |
| Actual RBO extracted today | ~10.5 LMT (verified, India #1) |
| **Extraction gap** | **~18 LMT** |
| Realistic capture (45–70% of gap, via stabilisation) | **+8 to +14 LMT edible RBO/yr** |
| × import parity ₹100–110/kg | **≈ ₹8,000 – 15,000 cr/yr displaced** |

**Central estimate: ~+10 LMT of additional edible rice-bran oil ≈ ₹10,500 cr/yr of imports
avoided — roughly 6% of the ~₹1.6–1.7 lakh cr edible-oil import bill (SEA 2024-25, verified),
from a byproduct India already generates and largely wastes.** No new acreage, no new water,
no new MSP outlay — only bran stabilisation and routing to the ~350 SEA solvent plants that
already exist ([domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md](domestic-procurement-stability-layer.md)).
This is the single most defensible import-substitution number in the whole oilseed debate —
and it dwarfs anything oilseed *procurement* delivers in-year (Q1: ≈ 0).

### 2. Biodiesel from residuals — real but secondary

The **non-edible** residual of RBO refining (rice-bran acid oil, soapstock, fatty-acid
distillate ≈ 6% of crude oil) transesterifies at ~90–93% (verified — ScienceDirect). On the
incremental crush that is only **~0.5–1.7 LMT biodiesel ≈ ₹450–1,500 cr/yr** of diesel
displaced. Plus **husk** — ~183 LMT on procured paddy alone — as boiler fuel/power (~0.9
MWh/t). **Note the asymmetry:** edible RBO earns ~₹100+/kg (import parity) vs biodiesel ~₹90/L.
**Biodiesel is the residual outlet, ~1/10th the edible-oil prize — never the primary use.**

### 3. Unit economics — RBO costs far less than the oil it replaces

Per tonne RBO (~6 t bran at ~16.7% oil):
- Bran feedstock at ₹15–19/kg (repo bran ₹19/kg) → ₹90,000–114,000/t, **less DORB (de-oiled
  bran) feed credit ~₹75,000/t**, + stabilise/extract/refine ~₹20,000–30,000/t
- **Ex-mill RBO cost ≈ ₹55–75/kg vs ₹100–110/kg import parity → gross margin ₹25–55/kg.**

The margin is **positive even if the state prices the bran at ₹19/kg instead of gifting it.**
So the current CMR arrangement — handing bran to millers free (the **₹7,654 cr/yr net
giveaway**, [omss-processing-returns.md](omss-processing-returns.md) Finding 3) — is
value-destroying twice over: the state forgoes both the feedstock revenue *and* the
import-substitution margin. Pricing bran into CMR auctions turns the ₹7,654 cr giveaway into
**feedstock revenue for the state** while still leaving the extractor a workable spread.

### 4. Scale of the integrated rice-biorefinery, and the incentive that would build it

From ~832 LMT government paddy: **558 LMT rice (the food obligation) + 183 LMT husk + 67 LMT
bran + ~24 LMT brokens.** The byproduct value pool is **~₹20,134 cr/yr gross (₹7,654 cr net)
on procured paddy alone** (verified, repo), scaling to **~₹54,000 cr/yr gross at national
paddy volume** (derived). Against that, raw milled-rice sale earns the state *no* margin (it
is welfare disposal). **The entire commercial upside of the grain book sits in the byproduct
cascade it currently gives away.**

**Incentive design — copy what made ethanol work, not what made biodiesel fail.** E20 succeeded
because the state created *guaranteed offtake* (blending mandate) + *capex support* (distillery
interest-subvention). The identical toolkit builds the bran chain:
- **Interest subvention** on bran-stabilisation + solvent-extraction + husk-power capex at
  mill scale (the proven distillery-loan template).
- **A demand mandate with teeth** — the RBO analogue is the **SAF 1%-2027→5%-2030 mandate**
  (verified) for the non-edible residual, and health-labelled public procurement (Bharat RBO)
  for the edible fraction.
- **Price the bran** into CMR auctions — converts the giveaway into feedstock revenue and a
  price signal that pulls private/co-op investment into stabilisation.
- **Priority-sector lending + viability-gap funding** for husk-power and silica units.

### 5. Start where the grain is already aggregated: the DFG/ethanol distillery

The sharpest integration insight. The **grain-ethanol industry already buys and cleans
damaged food grain (DFG)** — ethanol from DFG fetches **~₹64/L**, maize ~₹72/L, FCI surplus
rice ~₹60.32/L (verified — Business Standard Oct 2024; DFPD). **Cleaning the DFG — which the
distillery already does — mechanically separates sound starch (→ fermentation) from bran +
husk + fines.** So the distillery is *already the aggregation point that generates the bran
and husk streams.* Does it need them?
- **Husk — yes, structurally.** Grain distilleries need process heat and **already run rice-
  husk / biomass-fired boilers with co-gen** (verified — multiple grain-distillery
  environment-clearance DPRs: 50–55 TPH biomass boilers, 3.2–7 MW co-gen, husk the standard
  fuel). Husk-firing = energy self-sufficiency for the plant.
- **Bran — extract the oil *before* fermentation.** Bran carries ~16% oil; fermenting it
  sends high-value edible oil (~₹100/kg) into low-value ethanol. The efficient sequence is
  **clean DFG → extract bran oil (edible RBO, import substitution) → ferment the de-oiled
  starch → ethanol → DDGS (distillers grains) → animal feed → husk fires the boiler.** One
  facility, five products, **zero incremental food grain consumed** (DDGS is already the
  standard post-ferment feed byproduct — verified).

**Two different oils — do not conflate them.** There are *two* extraction points at a grain
plant, and India's recommendation hinges on getting the order right:

| | Classical rice-bran oil (RBO) | Distillers oil (DCO/DRO) |
|---|---|---|
| Where | **Front-end**: bran milled off *before* fermentation | **Back-end**: centrifuged from *post-ferment* thin/whole stillage |
| Plant | dedicated solvent-extraction (SEA ~30 MT capacity) | bolt-on centrifuge at the ethanol plant |
| Grade | **edible** (import substitution, ~₹100/kg) | non-edible process co-product (fuel/feed) |
| US practice | n/a | **>80% of US dry-grind plants extract it**; of DCO **~45% → biodiesel, ~51% → feed** (verified — grains.org Ch.3; RFA; SUAT — Annex IX Part B / RFS D4-D5) |
| India status | established industry, under-extracting | arriving — Indian grain-ethanol plants now list "corn oil" as a co-product alongside DDGS |

**Where India must diverge from the US model:** the US routes distillers oil mostly to
*biodiesel* because at corn scale it is a fuel feedstock. India should **not** copy
biodiesel-first — its bran oil is worth ~₹100/kg as **edible** import substitution vs ~₹90/L
as diesel. So the correct integrated cascade is **edible-first, fuel-from-residual**:

> **Front-end de-bran → extract EDIBLE bran oil first (import substitution) → ferment the
> de-oiled starch → ethanol → then route only the residual (rice-bran acid oil, DORB, and the
> back-end post-ferment distillers oil) to biodiesel; husk fires the boiler; DDGS to feed.**

**The distillery-as-aggregation-host thesis holds; the *ordering* is the India-specific
correction.** Bolting front-end bran-oil extraction onto a grain-ethanol plant captures the
edible-oil import-substitution prize (Part 1) at the one site where the feedstock is already
gathered and cleaned. The policy sequence writes itself: **start by cleaning the damaged food
grain the ethanol industry already markets, extract the *edible* oil first, and let ethanol +
husk-power + DDGS + non-edible residual biodiesel be the cascade around it** — the
food-vs-fuel trap avoided entirely, because the food grain (sound rice) is never the fuel.
