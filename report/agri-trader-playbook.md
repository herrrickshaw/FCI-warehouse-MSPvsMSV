# The Agri-Trader Playbook: How Global Merchants Monetize India's Agricultural Surplus — and What the Indian State Could Legitimately Emulate

**Date:** 23 July 2026
**Status:** Research report, web-sourced. Every India-specific factual claim is cited with URL and vintage. Claims that could not be verified are explicitly labelled. Company information sourced from marketing pages (vs. filings) is flagged as such.

---

## Executive summary

The ABCD majors (ADM, Bunge, Cargill, Louis Dreyfus) plus Olam, Wilmar and COFCO International do **not** make money by betting that grain prices will rise. They monetize four structural spreads — **origination basis** (buy at farmgate, sell at port), **processing spreads** (crush margin, milling margin), **storage optionality** (hold only when the futures curve pays for carry, always hedged), and **freight/geography arbitrage** — plus **volatility itself**, via hedged basis trading where futures losses and physical gains offset. In India, all seven run exactly this playbook: Cargill refines and brands edible oil (Gemini, NatureFresh) and originates ~1 Mt/yr of grain; Olam Agri is among India's largest rice exporters (~11.8% of Indian rice exports, ~600 kt, ~$2.5 bn); Wilmar just became sole promoter of AWL Agri Business (ex-Adani Wilmar, ₹63,672 cr FY25 revenue) after Adani's staged 2025 exit; LDC merchandizes cotton, rice and coffee; ITC's e-Choupal is the domestic analogue. They buy government surplus cheaply — FCI's OMSS e-auctions sell wheat/rice to millers, processors and traders at reserve prices near or below FCI's own economic cost.

The state-trader record is bimodal. COFCO built itself into a top-five global merchant by buying commercial trading houses (Nidera, Noble Agri, >$3 bn) and running them commercially at arm's length from Chinese domestic policy. Thailand's 2011–14 rice pledging scheme is the opposite pole: the state bought paddy ~40–50% above market to corner global supply, ended with losses credibly estimated at ฿450–620 bn (~US$13–19 bn) and rice rotting in warehouses. India's own STEs (STC, PEC) are non-operative shells being wound up; NAFED and CCI are loss-making MSP-implementation arms, not traders.

The legitimate emulation playbook for India is: (1) capture processing spreads via custom-milling/tolling contracts rather than state-owned mills (HAFED already outsources its own oil-mill crushing); (2) run storage as an optionality business — which first requires un-banning the hedging instruments, since SEBI's December 2021 suspension of seven agri futures (paddy, wheat, chana, mustard, soy complex, CPO, moong) was still in force through at least March 2026; (3) auction export quotas transparently instead of trading through NCEL's opaque nomination (NCEL executed only ~3–4% of allocated G2G volumes by April 2024); (4) fix basis/geography losses — FCI's economic cost of rice (~₹4,000+/qtl class) vs. OMSS realizations of ₹2,250–2,320/qtl is a negative spread a trader would never run; (5) scale the Bharat-brand downstream margin pilot (15.2 LMT atta + 14.6 LMT rice moved in Phase-I); and (6) never emulate naked directional accumulation — India's 36.3 Mt rice mountain (Oct 2025, 10-year high, 2.5× buffer norm) is structurally Thailand-shaped even if intentions differ. Institutionally, the COFCO lesson is separation: a commercially mandated sovereign trading arm can work only if it is not simultaneously the buffer-stock holder, the price-stabilization agent and the de-facto regulator.

Note the pattern in the 2024 numbers: revenues fell with prices, and **crush-margin compression — not price direction — drove the earnings decline** at ADM and Bunge. That is direct evidence of what the business actually is: a spread business.

### 1.2 The five margin engines

1. **Origination spread.** Buy at the farmgate/mandi, aggregate, condition, and sell at port or to a processor. The margin is the difference between a fragmented, information-poor buying market and a consolidated, information-rich selling market, minus logistics. ADM's own 10-K language says segment performance is "better evaluated by looking at margins since both revenues and cost of products sold generally have a relatively equal impact from market price changes" ([ADM 10-K FY2024](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/7084/000000708425000011/adm-20241231.htm)). The classic study of the model is Oxfam's *Cereal Secrets* (2012), which documents how the four firms span farm inputs to food manufacturing and profit at each stage ([Oxfam PDF](https://www-cdn.oxfam.org/s3fs-public/file_attachments/rr-cereal-secrets-grain-traders-agriculture-30082012-en_4.pdf)).

2. **Processing spreads.** Oilseed crush (bean → meal + oil), corn wet-milling (corn → starch/sweeteners/ethanol), wheat milling. These are quoted, hedgeable spreads (e.g., board crush). When 2024 soy crush margins collapsed in North/South America, Bunge's processing earnings fell ~60% ([OFI Magazine](https://www.ofimagazine.com/news/bunge-2024-results-impacted-by-weak-oilseed-processing-margins)) — again, the P&L lives in the spread.

3. **Storage optionality — carry, never naked length.** When futures curves are in contango (carry), the deferred price pays the trader to store hedged inventory: buy spot, sell the deferred future, earn the spread minus storage/finance cost, with price risk removed. US extension literature describes this exactly: "a strong carry occurs when there are excess supplies … future buyers are willing to pay farmers to take the risk of storage" ([Univ. of Wisconsin extension](https://farms.extension.wisc.edu/articles/understanding-basis-in-grain-marketing/)). The trader holds *hedged* inventory when the market pays for it, and runs inventory down when it doesn't. This is the single most important contrast with government stockholding, which is driven by procurement obligations regardless of what storage earns.

4. **Basis and calendar-spread trading.** Practitioner accounts of the ag-trading desks are blunt: "Most of the profits come from understanding the physical to make money on the futures side by trading calendar spreads, crush spreads or trading the Chinese vs the Chicago futures" ([Wall Street Oasis practitioner thread](https://www.wallstreetoasis.com/forum/trading/best-agricultural-trading-firm-abcd) — anecdotal source, but consistent with the segment accounting above). Because the firms are long physical and short futures (or vice versa), they monetize **volatility and basis dislocations**, not direction. High-volatility years (2021–22) produced record ABCD profits; the calm, low-price 2024 produced the declines shown above.

5. **Freight, logistics and risk services.** Ports, barges and chartering desks are profit centers, and so is selling risk management itself (structured pricing contracts to farmers and food companies). Academic literature describes the evolution "from grain merchants to agricultural value chain managers" ([ResearchGate, 2015](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283198586_ABCD_and_beyond_From_grain_merchants_to_agricultural_value_chain_managers)).

**Asset-light origination:** the network of country elevators, agents and franchised buying (like ITC's e-Choupal, §2.7) means the trader's capital sits in *chokepoint* assets (ports, crush plants) while origination is largely rented. Oxfam's *Cereal Secrets* and openDemocracy's ABCD survey ([link](https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/oureconomy/abcd-grain-giants-profit-world-hunger/)) both document the ~75–90% share of world grain trade attributed to the four majors (the 90% figure is an oft-repeated estimate, not an audited number — label: **estimate**).

## 3. State traders: the success, the catastrophe, and India's own record

### 3.1 COFCO — the emulation success

- China's state food group bought **51% of Nidera (2014)** — alongside HOPU, Temasek, IFC, StanChart PE — took Nidera to 100% (announced Aug 2016, completed Feb 2017), and acquired **Noble Agri to 100%**; total spend **>$3 bn**, deliberately to "transform the Chinese player into a rival to the ABCDs" ([World Grain, Mar 2014](https://www.world-grain.com/articles/4005-cofco-buys-51-of-nidera), [Feb 2017](https://www.world-grain.com/articles/7793-cofco-completes-acquisition-of-nidera); [COFCO Intl "Our story"](https://www.cofcointernational.com/who-we-are/our-story/)).
- Result: Geneva-based COFCO International, ~12,400 staff in ~36 countries, 108 Mt volumes, $38.5 bn revenue in a weak 2024 (§1.1) — a genuine top-five-class merchant by volume.
- **Why it worked (analysis):** (a) it *bought* existing commercial organisms — trading books, talent, port assets — rather than asking a procurement bureaucracy to learn trading; (b) it is structurally separated from domestic price policy: Chinese domestic reserves are run by Sinograin/state reserves, while COFCO Intl trades internationally on commercial terms; (c) minority co-investors (Temasek, IFC, HOPU) imposed commercial governance in the formative years; (d) it hedges — it is a basis trader like its peers, not a stockpile.

### 3.2 Thailand's rice pledging scheme (2011–14) — the cautionary tale

- Mechanism: the Yingluck government "pledged" (bought) paddy from farmers at ~40–50% above market, expecting to corner world supply and resell high. World prices did not comply (Vietnam/India expanded exports — India lifted its own rice export ban in 2011).
- Verified damage figures (they vary by scope — cite carefully):
  - Government's own mid-2013 admission: **฿136 bn (~US$4.4 bn) lost in the 2011-12 season alone** ([Fox News/AP, 2013](https://www.foxnews.com/world/thailand-admits-losing-4-4bn-from-controversial-rice-scheme); [Library of Congress note, Aug 2013](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2013-08-07/thailand-crisis-in-thai-rice-pledging-scheme/)).
  - Full-scheme estimates: **"up to ฿500 bn"** losses; total estimated loss range **฿450–620 bn** (~US$13–19 bn at contemporary rates) ([The Nation (Thailand), gov't spokesman Anucha](https://www.nationthailand.com/in-focus/40009917); [Thai Examiner, May 2025](https://www.thaiexaminer.com/thai-news-foreigners/2025/05/22/still-hope-for-yingluck-after-supreme-court-sets-10-billion-baht-civil-liability-lawyer-cites-new-evidence/)).
  - Court-assessed personal liability: Supreme Administrative Court ordered Yingluck to pay **฿10.028 bn (~US$305 m)**, May 2025 ([Bangkok Post](https://www.bangkokpost.com/thailand/general/3031880/ex-pm-yingluck-ordered-to-pay-b10bn-for-thai-rice-scheme-losses); [The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2025/05/thai-court-orders-former-pm-yingluck-shinawatra-to-pay-rice-scheme-damages/)).
  - Physical rot: **>200,000 t of rice left rotting**, some unfit even for animal feed; ~฿100 bn debt piled on the agricultural bank BAAC ([The Nation](https://www.nationthailand.com/in-focus/40009917)).
- **Lesson:** the scheme was a naked directional long — above-market acquisition, no hedge, no exit mechanism, storage as an afterthought — executed at sovereign scale. Every element is the inverse of §1.2.

### 3.3 Other state traders

- **BULOG (Indonesia):** stabilization buyer/importer, not a profit-seeking trader; runs price-stabilization operations and premium-rice trading; 2024-25 import tenders of 320–500 kt routinely awarded to Vietnamese/Thai/Myanmar/Pakistani sellers ([S&P Global tender coverage](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/agriculture/092524-indonesias-bulog-awards-eighth-rice-tender-to-myanmar-vietnam-thailand-pakistan-wins-8-lots)). Governance risk is live: BULOG/Bapanas were reported to Indonesia's anti-corruption commission (KPK) over alleged **markups on Vietnamese rice imports** ([Katadata/Databoks, 2024](https://databoks.katadata.co.id/en/agroindustry/statistics/ba97f9e81102f27/vietnam-rice-markup-issue-whats-the-latest-trend-in-indonesian-rice-imports)). A state importer that buys at tender is exactly where rent-seeking concentrates.
- **Vinafood I / II (Vietnam):** state exporters that win those BULOG tenders ([VietnamPlus, 2024](https://en.vietnamplus.vn/vietnamese-companies-win-indonesian-rice-contracts/279179.vnp)) — a functioning state-exporter model, but one operating in a country with an exportable-surplus mandate and hard export-revenue incentives, not a consumer-price-suppression mandate.
- **SALIC (Saudi Arabia):** rather than building a trader, bought one — 80.01% of Olam Agri (§2.5). This is the *acquisition* variant of the COFCO route, executed by a food-importing state.
- **India's own STEs:**
  - **STC:** "has stopped undertaking any new business activity and is currently continuing as a non-operative company" ([STC corporate site](https://stclimited.co.in/)); commerce ministry moved to close STC and PEC (2019 reports: [Business Standard](https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/pti-stories/commerce-ministry-to-close-stc-pec-119091601443_1.html)); in Sept 2022 MMTC/STC/PEC were **denotified as canalising agencies**; closure of all three "under consideration" per the ministry, with MMTC's disinvestment undecided ([PSU Watch](https://psuwatch.com/latestnews/govt-clarifies-on-status-of-strategic-disinvestment-of-mmtc); [DTNext](https://www.dtnext.in/news/business/high-level-meeting-likely-to-take-call-on-winding-up-mmtc-stc-pec-741892)). Label: winding-down is verified in direction, **not yet a completed formal closure** in the sources found.
  - **NAFED:** MSP price-support (PSS) implementation arm for pulses/oilseeds; carries buffer stocks (10.11 LMT pulses in PSF buffer as of 02-Dec-2024; ~75.86 LMT disposed in Phase 2 incl. welfare channels), sells discounted chana to states at ₹8/kg markdowns; had to sign a ₹478 cr debt settlement with 8 banks (2018) after earlier trading losses ([Year-End Review DoCA 2024](https://worldtradescanner.com/2024%20Year-End-Review%20for%20Department%20of%20Consumer%20Affairs.htm); [Business Standard, 2018](https://www.business-standard.com/amp/article/pti-stories/nafed-signs-rs-478-cr-debt-settlement-pact-with-8-banks-118032800876_1.html)).
  - **CCI (cotton):** 2024-25 season procured **100 lakh bales (~38% of arrivals, ~34% of production)** at MSP, then faced "lukewarm" resale response on old stock ([PIB, Apr 2025](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2120025); [Fibre2Fashion](https://www.fibre2fashion.com/news/raw-material-price-news/india-s-cci-procures-32-5-lakh-bales-cotton-puts-pressure-on-mills-307161-newsdetails.htm)). CCI buys above market by construction (that is the MSP's purpose) and sells into a market it has itself tightened — a structural negative spread.

### 3.4 Why India's STEs failed where COFCO succeeded (analysis)

1. **Mandate:** COFCO Intl's mandate is commercial return + supply security; STC/MMTC were *canalising* monopolies whose rents vanished when trade liberalized; NAFED/CCI's mandate is to lose money gracefully (buy above market, dispose below).
2. **Instruments:** COFCO hedges on CBOT/Euronext/DCE. Indian agencies cannot hedge domestically — the state suspended the very futures contracts (paddy, wheat, chana, mustard, soy) it would need (§4.2).
3. **People and P&L:** COFCO bought trading organisms with retained talent and trader-style compensation; Indian STEs are staffed and paid as PSUs, with vigilance regimes that punish any loss-making trade ex post — which makes basis trading (where individual legs lose by design) institutionally impossible.
4. **Separation:** COFCO Intl is not the agency that runs China's domestic buffer or sets its floor prices. FCI/NAFED/CCI are simultaneously buffer-holder, price-stabilizer and market participant — every commercial action is contaminated by a policy objective, and counterparties know it (they front-run OMSS announcements and MSP-season behavior).

## 4. The emulation playbook for the Indian state — what is legitimate, concrete, and legal

### 4.0 The raw material: India's surplus is real and growing

- FCI rice stocks hit a **10-year high of 36.3 Mt (Oct 2025), 2.5× the buffer norm**; central pool foodgrains 67.6 Mt on 1-Oct-2025; annual rice procurement 52–53 Mt vs. welfare requirement 36–38 Mt — a structural ~15 Mt/yr surplus accumulation ([Rice News Today](https://ricenewstoday.com/rice-stocks-with-fci-surge-to-10-year-high/); [Igrain, Oct 2025](https://www.igrain.in/posts/fcis-central-pool-stocks-strong-in-october-2025)). Only 4.19 Mt was offloaded at subsidized rates in 2025-26 to that point ([SEFPL summary](https://www.sefpl.com/news/fcis-rice-stocks-surge-to-10-year-high-raising-cost-and-storage-concerns/)).
- The disposal spread is deeply negative: OMSS rice reserve price was cut to **₹2,250/qtl** for states/ethanol makers and **₹2,320/qtl for ethanol** in the 2026-27 policy — *below paddy MSP*, let alone FCI's economic cost ([Rural Voice, 2026](https://eng.ruralvoice.in/national/government-to-sell-fci-rice-for-ethanol-at-rs-2320-per-qtl-below-paddy-msp-omss-policy-2026-27-announced.html); [Deccan Herald — states can buy at ₹2,800/qtl](https://www.deccanherald.com/india/states-can-buy-rice-directly-from-fci-at-rs-2800-per-quintal-pralhad-joshi-3133153)). Every tonne disposed crystallizes a loss a trader would have refused to book by never carrying unhedged, purpose-free inventory.

### 4.1 Crush/processing spread capture — do it by tolling, not by owning

- **Precedent already exists inside the system.** HAFED (Haryana's apex co-op federation) runs two mustard oil mills (Rewari, est. 1986; Narnaul, 30 t/day, est. 2003), rice mills, and a turmeric plant (Radaur, ₹7.6 cr, 3 t/day) — and, tellingly, decided as early as 2004-05 to run its rice mills "in association with private millers for better capacity utilization and profitability," and now **tenders out the entire crushing/packing/labour operation of its oil mills** ([HAFED oil mills](http://hafed.gov.in/oil-mills); [HAFED rice mills](http://hafed.gov.in/rice-mills-0); [HAFED turmeric plant](http://hafed.gov.in/turmeric-plant); HAFED's own outsourcing tender notice via its [Facebook post](https://www.facebook.com/HAFEDharyana/posts/680721357625209/)).
- **Playbook:** the state agency should own the *grain* and buy the *milling service* (custom milling / tolling), capturing the crush or milling spread without operating plants. FCI already does this for paddy→rice via custom-milled rice; extend the same logic to (a) surplus-year mustard/soy crush for the PSF pulse-oil buffer, (b) maize→ethanol/starch tolling in record-crop years (the 55 Mt maize crop and ethanol demand, §2.9, make this spread liquid). Legality: procurement of services by tender is standard; no new statute needed.

### 4.2 Storage optionality done right — first, un-ban the hedge

- **The critical constraint, verified:** SEBI suspended derivatives in **seven agri commodities — paddy (non-basmati), wheat, chana, mustard seed complex, soybean complex, crude palm oil, moong** — in December 2021 (chana and mustard earlier in 2021), and extended the suspension repeatedly: to Dec 2023, Dec 2024, Jan 2025, then **31 Mar 2025**, then **31 Mar 2026** ([Business Standard, Jan 2025](https://www.business-standard.com/amp/markets/news/sebi-extends-futures-trading-ban-on-seven-agri-commodities-till-march-31-125013101629_1.html); [Zee Business explainer](https://www.zeebiz.com/markets/commodities/news-trading-in-wheat-chana-mustard-seeds-4-other-agri-commodities-suspended-for-additional-year-key-things-to-know-352275)).
- **Status as of mid-2026: not cleanly verifiable.** As of early 2026 SEBI was publicly reviewing the commodity-derivatives framework and "hinting at lifting" the ban ([Rural Voice](https://eng.ruralvoice.in/national/sebi-extends-futures-trading-ban-in-7-agri-commodities-till-january-reconsideration.html); [AgriTimes on the SEBI review](https://agritimes.co.in/amp/commodities-exports/sebi-may-revise-agricultural-commodity-classification-margin-rules-to-deepen-derivatives-market)), but **no notification confirming resumption after 31 Mar 2026 was found in this research. Treat "which contracts are live in July 2026" as UNVERIFIED and check SEBI's site before acting on it.** Live agri contracts in recent years have included castor, guar complex, cotton/kapas, turmeric, jeera, coriander (NCDEX/MCX) — also to be re-verified against current exchange lists.
- **Playbook once instruments exist:** the buffer above the norm (the ~2.5× excess, §4.0) should be run as a **carry book**: hold only when the deferred price covers storage + finance (sell futures/forwards against inventory), and disgorge when the curve inverts. Even without domestic futures, proxies exist: (a) *deferred-delivery e-auctions* (sell OMSS stock today for staggered future lifting at premia — capturing carry contractually), and (b) minimum-price-guaranteed warehouse-receipt lending against e-NWRs so private trade carries the stock instead. The deep irony to state plainly: **the government banned the hedging tools a rational public stockholder needs; the first step of "storage optionality done right" is regulatory, not operational.**

### 4.3 Export arbitrage — auction the quota, don't trade the grain

- India already exports surplus by fiat: after the Sep-2024 lifting of the rice ban (§2.9), and via **G2G allocations** — 1.93 Mt of non-basmati white rice, broken rice, sugar, wheat, onion approved to Bhutan, Bangladesh, Côte d'Ivoire, Egypt, Kenya, Nepal, Philippines, Guinea, Singapore, UAE, plus 2 LMT broken rice to Indonesia — channelled through **NCEL** ([Business Standard, Dec 2023](https://www.business-standard.com/industry/agriculture/govt-permits-ncel-to-export-900k-tonnes-rice-35k-tonnes-wheat-products-123120400715_1.html); [Gleaf/April-2024 execution data](https://www.gleaf.in/news/india-supplies-only-37-of-export-restricted-food-commodities-to-countries-committed-two-years-ago)).
- **NCEL's record argues against the state-trader route:** by end-April 2024 it had supplied only **3.9% of allocated non-basmati rice, 3.2% of broken rice, 3% of wheat, 0% of sugar** ([Gleaf](https://www.gleaf.in/news/india-supplies-only-37-of-export-restricted-food-commodities-to-countries-committed-two-years-ago)). NCEL (registered Jan 2023; promoters IFFCO, KRIBHCO, NAFED, GCMMF/Amul, NCDC; ₹500 cr paid-up) claims ₹7,000 cr orders and paid a 20% dividend in 2023-24 ([Vajiram explainer](https://vajiramandravi.com/current-affairs/ncel/); [PIB doc, Sep 2025](https://static.pib.gov.in/WriteReadData/specificdocs/documents/2025/sep/doc2025926647401.pdf)) — but its monopoly nomination for restricted-commodity exports is a discretionary allocation of scarcity rent.
- **Playbook:** when exports are restricted, the export *right* itself has market value. Auction tradeable export quotas (as with sugar export quotas historically) instead of nominating a single cooperative: the state captures the scarcity rent transparently at auction, private traders (Olam, LDC, ITC, Indian houses) execute the logistics they are demonstrably better at, and the audit trail is public. Legality: quota auctions are administratively established practice (DGFT notifications); no statutory obstacle.

### 4.4 Basis/geography arbitrage — fix the negative internal spread

- FCI's mandated zone-transfer movements (surplus north-west → deficit east/south) are executed at rail freight + handling costs that the Shanta Kumar Committee (2015) found systematically above private benchmarks; the committee recommended outsourcing stocking to CWC/SWC/private warehouses, cash transfers in million-plus cities, and pruning NFSA coverage from 67% to 40%, and estimated PDS leakage at ~46–47% (contested; World Bank estimated ~35% by 2011-12) ([Shanta Kumar Committee — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanta_Kumar_Committee); [GKToday summary](https://www.gktoday.in/shanta-kumar-committee-recommendations/); [India Together critique](https://indiatogether.org/shanta-kumar-committee-report-on-pds-leakage-and-food-security-act-government)).
- The storage-outsourcing half is already happening — as a private bonanza rather than a state margin: **Adani Agri Logistics and LEAP India won 110 of 134 FCI silo contracts worth >₹16,500 cr** (~46.5 of 60 LMT modern silo capacity), with FCI's own proposed anti-monopoly clause removed along the way ([Newslaundry investigation, May 2026](https://www.newslaundry.com/2026/05/29/anti-monopoly-clause-gone-adanis-footprint-grew-across-indias-grain-storage-programme); [Adani Ports release](https://www.adaniports.com/newsroom/media-releases/adani-agri-logistics-secures-loa-from-fci-for-silo-complexes)).
- **Playbook:** publish FCI's zone-transfer cost per tonne-km against private benchmarks; competitively tender end-to-end movement corridors (not just storage); restore anti-concentration clauses in silo tendering. The "margin" the state captures here is cost avoided, which — unlike trading profit — no mandate forbids.

### 4.5 Downstream brand margin — scale the Bharat pilot honestly

- Verified pilot scale: **Bharat Atta ₹27.50/kg launch (Nov 2023), Bharat Rice ₹29/kg (Feb 2024)**; Phase-I moved **~15.20 LMT atta and 14.58 LMT rice**; Phase-II (from Oct-Nov 2024) at ₹30/kg atta, ₹34/kg rice, with 3.69 LMT wheat + 2.91 LMT rice initially allocated via NAFED, NCCF and Kendriya Bhandar; Bharat chana dal at ₹60-70/kg ([PIB, Feb 2024](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleaseIframePage.aspx?PRID=2003160); [PIB Phase-II, Nov 2024](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2070798); [ANI](https://aninews.in/news/business/subsidized-bharat-brand-rice-wheat-being-made-available-to-consumers-in-phase-ii20241105144642/)).
- Today Bharat is a *price-suppression* channel (sold below cost). The ABCD lesson (Cargill's Gemini/NatureFresh, AWL's Fortune) is that milling + packing + brand converts commodity into 15–30% gross-margin FMCG. A **commercial second tier** — market-priced Bharat SKUs in deficit regions, with the subsidized tier ring-fenced — would let FCI/NAFED capture processing-and-brand margin on surplus stock without new legislation. What to avoid: using below-cost Bharat SKUs to undercut private millers while simultaneously selling them OMSS wheat — that is the market-maker/competitor conflict of §4.7.

### 4.6 G2G deals — keep, but price honestly

India's G2G channel exists (allocations to 10+ countries, §4.3). Its legitimate uses are diplomacy and guaranteed offtake for surplus. Its illegitimate use is hiding a below-cost disposal inside an opaque bilateral price. Playbook: benchmark every G2G price against the FOB market on signing date and publish the implied subsidy, exactly as the OMSS reserve-price gap is publishable. (BULOG's KPK referral, §3.3, shows where opaque state-to-state pricing leads.)

### 4.7 What NOT to emulate

1. **Naked directional holding (Thailand).** India's rice mountain is already a passive version of it: bought above market (MSP), held unhedged, sold below cost. The difference from Thailand is intent (welfare vs. profit) and the absence of a resale-price bet — but the balance-sheet mechanics converge. The fix is flow discipline (procure-to-need + carry-book disposal), not better trading.
2. **Opaque cross-subsidies.** NAFED's ₹8/kg discounted chana transfers, sub-MSP ethanol rice at ₹2,320/qtl, and unpublished G2G pricing all bury losses that should appear as explicit budget lines. Traders mark to market daily; a state trader that cannot publish a daily mark should not trade.
3. **Monopoly nomination as a substitute for capability.** NCEL's 3–4% execution rate (§4.3) shows a nominated monopoly without trading infrastructure captures rent without performing the function.
4. **The regulator-trader-buffer trinity.** The entity that sets/administers MSP and stock limits must not also be the profit-seeking trader; counterparties will price its policy information advantage into every trade (and courts/CAG will treat every losing hedge leg as a scandal).

### 4.8 Institutional form (synthesis)

The COFCO/SALIC evidence says: **buy or JV into commercial capability; don't mandate it onto FCI/NAFED.** A workable Indian design, consistent with everything verified above:

- A **sovereign agri-trading company** (COFCO model) with: (i) a commercial mandate and board, minority anchor co-investors for governance discipline (as Temasek/IFC were for Nidera); (ii) *no* role in MSP, buffer norms, or OMSS policy; (iii) authority to hedge on domestic and international exchanges (contingent on SEBI restoring the contracts, §4.2); (iv) first-refusal access to above-norm central-pool surplus at transparent transfer prices — the surplus becomes its originated inventory, priced in, not free.
- FCI remains the welfare-logistics utility (procure, store to norm, distribute NFSA), shedding above-norm stock to the trading arm or to quota-auctioned exports.
- NAFED/CCI remain PSS executors, but with disposal handed to the trading arm's carry book instead of ad-hoc discounted sales.
- Hard guardrails: statutory loss limits per book, daily mark-to-market published with a lag, CAG audit of *process* (was it hedged? was the auction competitive?) rather than *outcome* (did this leg lose?) — without which no Indian public entity can rationally run a hedged book.

## 5. The buyer's-eye view — the US Grains Council on India vs its other markets

The US Grains Council (USGC, recently restyled "U.S. Grains & BioProducts Council") is the producer-country mirror image of everything in §1–§4: a demand-creation agency for US corn, sorghum, barley, DDGS and ethanol, with full-time presence in ~28 locations worldwide and a stated doctrine of working "where the market does not." How it characterizes India is a useful external audit of India's own grain-trade posture.

### 5.1 USGC's India assessment

- **The single biggest access issue is GM policy.** India bans GM corn imports and cultivation; only non-GM corn can enter, and only under government-granted quotas — while ~94% of US corn is GM. USGC and S&P coverage treat this as the structural barrier, ahead of tariffs ([S&P Global, Oct 2025](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/agriculture/100225-indias-grain-sector-resists-us-corn-imports-amid-trade-negotiations); [USGC India market profile](https://grains.org/markets-tools-data/markets/market-profiles/india/)).
- **Tariff wall + TRQ.** In-quota: **15% on 500,000 t** of maize (TRQ operated for poultry/starch end-users, DGFT-administered since 2019-20). Out-of-quota: **50% per S&P (Oct 2025); Reuters-derived coverage cites 60%** as the normal duty — the discrepancy likely reflects basic duty (50%) vs duty-plus-cess; label the exact out-of-quota rate as **source-dependent, verify against the current customs notification** ([S&P Global](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/agriculture/100225-indias-grain-sector-resists-us-corn-imports-amid-trade-negotiations); [Poultry World/Reuters](https://www.poultryworld.net/health-nutrition/india-corn-import-tariffs-reduced-to-boost-poultry-sector/); [DGFT TRQ notification via WorldTradeScanner, Jun 2020](http://worldtradescanner.com/Ntfn%2028-23.06.2020.htm)).
- **Ethanol is USGC's wedge into India.** India hit ~19.98% blending (E20) ahead of its Oct 2026 target; USGC frames the E20 program as a corn-demand story with a feedstock deficit, and has pitched a "two-tier procurement" model (imported ethanol as filler for domestic shortfall) plus open access to global corn for ethanol feedstock. India was the **fifth-largest US ethanol market in MY2022/23** and by MY2024/25 the **third-largest, with US share of India's ethanol imports at ~97%** ([USGC delegation report](https://grains.org/u-s-delegation-visits-india-to-search-for-new-outlets-for-u-s-ethanol/); [USGC ATM/Biofuels Summit report](https://grains.org/atm-ethanol-summit-in-india-grow-councils-reach-in-country/); [The Wire/CSTEP analysis, 2025](https://m.thewire.in/article/economy/us-india-trade-turns-a-corn-er-implications-for-indias-ethanol-policy)). 2025 trade-talk reports of a consensus to allow US corn imports **for ethanol only** are press-reported, resisted by Indian industry, and **not verified as a concluded agreement** ([S&P Global, Oct 2025](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/agriculture/100225-indias-grain-sector-resists-us-corn-imports-amid-trade-negotiations)).
- **DDGS: a >700,000 t/yr potential market blocked by technical barriers.** USGC's own developing-markets pieces put India's US-DDGS import potential above 700 kt/yr, with aquaculture (India is the world's third-largest farmed-fish producer) as the entry segment; feeding trials and sustainability documentation (CSAP) are the tools ([USGC — Developing Markets: DDGS in India](https://grains.org/developing-markets-council-strives-to-make-inroads-for-ddgs-in-india/); [World Grain, 2024](https://www.world-grain.com/articles/22996-indian-aquaculture-producers-strengthen-ties-to-us-feed-grains)). Sorghum/barley: no India-specific USGC program of comparable depth surfaced in this research — label: **thin coverage, not verified as a priority**.

### 5.2 Where India sits vs USGC's other markets

India is an **emerging/frontier market worked for future access**, not a volume market. USGC opened a **New Delhi office only in January 2023** ([Business Standard/ANI](https://www.business-standard.com/content/press-releases-ani/u-s-grains-council-india-office-opens-in-new-delhi-123011100897_1.html)), and its annual-meeting agendas class India with Africa under "emerging markets" ([USGC annual meeting](https://grains.org/emerging-markets-in-india-africa-ethanol-focus-of-u-s-grains-council-annual-meeting/)). Contrast the free-flow markets: **Mexico bought a record 25.5 Mt of US corn in MY2024/25 (35% of all US corn exports), Japan 13.3 Mt, Colombia 7.6 Mt**, with Korea/EU posting the largest y/y increases ([USGC 2025 annual report](https://grains.org/2025-annual-report/panels/u-s-corn-exports-up-nearly-27-percent-breaking-record/); [USGC 2024 annual report — Mexico record](https://grains.org/2024-annual-report/panels/mexico-breaks-record-for-u-s-corn-imports/)). In Mexico/Japan/Korea/SEA the Council does trade servicing and end-use expansion (feed, aqua, ethanol blending); in India it does **policy-barrier removal and pre-commercial groundwork** — ethanol first (no GM issue in a fuel molecule), DDGS second (co-product, sustainability-documented), corn last (blocked by GM + TRQ). Note the strategy's shape: when the commodity is blocked, sell the *derivative* of the commodity. Placement within USGC's formal Unified Export Strategy rankings is **not publicly verifiable** — the UES is a USDA funding document, not a published league table; the office-opening date and "emerging" framing are the observable proxies.

### 5.3 The inverse playbook: does India have a USGC for its own surplus?

USGC's model is: permanent in-market offices, buyer teams flown to origin, feeding trials and technical servicing that change *formulations* (demand creation, not just promotion), commodity-specific checkoff funding, and decade-long persistence against one named barrier at a time. India's nearest equivalent, **APEDA** (statutory, Dept. of Commerce, est. 1986), does infrastructure/quality/market-development assistance, buyer-seller meets via Indian missions, and a millets Export Promotion Forum + portal ([APEDA](https://apeda.gov.in/about-us); [PIB on millet promotion, Feb 2024](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2003546)). The gaps against the USGC template: APEDA is **generalist** (everything from basmati to buffalo meat) where USGC is commodity-deep; APEDA has **no permanent overseas market offices** comparable to USGC's 28 (it works through embassies); it runs **no technical demand-creation programs** (no feeding trials putting Indian maize/DDGS/rice-bran into Vietnamese or Bangladeshi feed formulations); and it is funded by government grant rather than a producer checkoff, so no constituency audits its results. A serious Indian "Grains Council" for rice/maize/millets would copy the mechanics: offices in Dhaka, Hanoi, Jakarta, Manila, Nairobi, Riyadh; feed-trial programs for Indian maize and de-oiled rice bran in SEA aqua feed (the mirror image of what USGC is doing *to* India's shrimp-feed market); ethanol-policy servicing in Africa for Indian ethanol/machinery; and a standing barrier-removal docket (e.g., Indonesia's rice-import STE channel, Bangladesh's fumigation rules). The asymmetry to state plainly: **USGC maintains a funded office in New Delhi working to open India's market; India maintains no equivalent office in any of its top grain-export destinations.** (Verified for USGC; the India-side absence is an inference from APEDA's published structure — label: inference, high confidence.)
