# How Other Countries Support Farmers and Manage Surplus — and What India's MSP-Procurement Model Could Borrow

**A comparative analysis of farm-support / price-vs-income architectures across the US, EU, China, Brazil, Indonesia, Egypt, Japan, South Korea, Turkey, Zambia and Malawi — read against India's MSP-procurement-buffer system.**

*July 2026. Companion to [`msp-vs-msv-main-report.md`](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md) (the MSP-vs-MSV evaluation and the recommended hybrid architecture in §5) and [`pricing-msp-msv-published.html`](pricing-msp-msv-published.html). This report is **distinct from** [`agri-trader-playbook.md`](agri-trader-playbook.md), which covers state TRADING arms (COFCO, SALIC, Thailand's rice-pledging catastrophe, the ABCD merchants). Where that brief asks "how does a state monetise surplus in the market," this one asks "how does a state deliver a price or income floor to farmers, and dispose of the resulting surplus, WITHOUT India's open-ended physical procurement." Every load-bearing figure carries a source URL, a vintage, and a **verified / reported / unverified** label.*

**Labelling convention.** *Verified* = confirmed across ≥2 independent sources or a primary government/CBO/OECD/WTO document. *Reported* = a single credible secondary source (trade press, one think-tank note, one USDA-FAS GAIN report). *Unverified* = my synthesis or inference, not a directly sourced fact.

## 0. The Indian baseline this report compares against

To read the comparisons, hold India's model fixed (all from the [main report](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md), verified there):

- **Instrument:** Minimum Support Price (MSP) announced for ~23 crops, but *de facto* a two-crop program — sustained physical procurement exists only for **rice and wheat** (KMS 2024-25 coarse-grain procurement was ~11.7 LMT vs 832 LMT of paddy, ~70× smaller).
- **Mechanism:** an **administered price backed by open-ended physical purchase** by FCI/state agencies, stored in a public buffer, disposed through PDS/NFSA.
- **Fiscal scale:** food subsidy **₹2.03 lakh crore allocated FY26** (₹1.99 lakh crore released FY25); central-pool stocks **476.85 LMT (1 Jan 2025)**, far above buffer norms.
- **The reform debate:** a universal legal MSP for 14 crops modelled at **≈₹3.40 lakh crore/year** (IEG WP420); the income-support alternative (enhanced PM-KISAN covering half of cultivation cost) at **≈₹1.22 lakh crore/year** — about a third of universal MSP.
- **WTO exposure:** India has invoked the WTO **peace clause five times** for rice, most recently for **2022-23**, having breached the **10% de-minimis** trade-distorting-support ceiling on rice. *(Verified — [Civilsdaily/WTO](https://www.wto.org/english/tratop_e/agric_e/factsheet_agng_e.htm), 2013 Bali Decision; India's own AMS notifications.)*

The central finding of the comparative literature: **India is running the exact model (administered price + open-ended procurement + accumulating public stock) that the EU built in the 1970s–80s, drowned in, and deliberately dismantled — and that China built in 2008–13 and partially abandoned in 2014–16.** Almost every other large system has moved the price *floor* off the government's balance sheet, either into decoupled income payments, deficiency payments, or market-based instruments.

## 1. United States — the income-support / deficiency-payment model (NO price-support procurement)

**(a) Core instrument.** The US does *not* physically buy grain to support prices. It runs four overlapping instruments:

1. **Price Loss Coverage (PLC)** — a per-base-acre **deficiency payment** triggered when the national marketing-year average price falls below a **statutory reference price**. Reference prices (2018 Farm Bill): **corn $3.70/bu, wheat $5.50/bu, soybeans $8.40/bu, seed cotton $0.367/lb**, with an "effective reference price" escalator capped at 115% of statutory. *(Verified — [farmdoc daily 2019](https://farmdocdaily.illinois.edu/2019/09/weekly-farm-economics-the-price-loss-coverage-plc-option-in-the-2018-farm-bill.html); CRS R45730.)* The pending 2024/25 reauthorisation raises these to **corn $4.10, wheat $6.35, soybeans $10.00, seed cotton $0.42**. *(Reported — [CRS R48167](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R48167.html), 2024.)*
2. **Agriculture Risk Coverage (ARC)** — a per-base-acre payment triggered when **county revenue** falls below a moving historical benchmark (revenue insurance logic, not a fixed price). *(Verified — CBO/FSA.)*
3. **Marketing Assistance Loans (MALs) + Loan Deficiency Payments (LDPs)** — the CCC **loan-rate mechanism**, and the single most India-relevant idea here. A farmer pledges the crop to the Commodity Credit Corporation for a nonrecourse loan at a per-unit **loan rate**; if the market price falls below the loan rate, the farmer repays at the lower market rate (or takes an LDP and forgoes the loan) and keeps the difference. This delivers a **price floor WITHOUT the government taking title to, storing, or disposing of the grain** in the normal case — "intended to minimize potential delivery of loan collateral to CCC, accumulation of CCC-owned stocks, storage costs." *(Verified — [USDA FSA](https://www.fsa.usda.gov/resources/price-support/loan-deficiency-payments); FSA non-recourse loan factsheets.)*
4. **Federally subsidised crop insurance** — now the **largest** farm-safety-net line (premium subsidy ~60% on average), a revenue/yield product delivered through private insurers. *(Verified — [NSAC](https://sustainableagriculture.net/blog/2018-farm-bill-commodities/); the 2014 Farm Bill "expanded the largest farm subsidy program — crop insurance.")*

**(b) How it differs from Indian MSP-procurement.** Fundamentally: the US floor is a **cash transfer of the price gap**, not a purchase. When the corn price falls, Washington writes cheques (PLC/LDP) or pays insurance indemnities — it does **not** accumulate a corn mountain. The grain stays in commercial channels; there is no PDS-scale public buffer for price support (the US strategic reserves are small and humanitarian, not a domestic price tool). The **2014 Farm Bill deliberately ended the old fixed Direct Payments and Counter-Cyclical Payments** and replaced them with the price/revenue-triggered PLC/ARC — a move *toward* counter-cyclicality and *away* from blanket payments. *(Verified — [LegalClarity/NSAC](https://sustainableagriculture.net/blog/2018-farm-bill-commodities/), 2014.)* This is precisely the **deficiency-payment destination India debates** (the IEG WP420 "30% procurement + deficiency payment on the rest" model).

**(c) Fiscal scale / coverage.** The Farm Bill baseline is **~$1.42 trillion over 10 years (FY2025–34)**, but **~80% is nutrition (SNAP)**, not farm support. *(Verified — [NSAC/CBO Feb 2024](https://sustainableagriculture.net/blog/cbos-farm-bill-baseline-explained/).)* Within farm support: **commodity programs (ARC/PLC/DMC/disaster) ~$57 billion over FY2025–34**; **crop insurance is the single biggest farm line** (net outlays estimated >$8 billion/year, ~$100 billion/decade). *(Verified — [CBO June 2024 baseline](https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-06/51317-2024-06-usda.pdf); [farmpolicynews 2024](https://farmpolicynews.illinois.edu/2024/02/crop-insurance-costs-projected-to-jump-29/).)* Coverage is broad among *commercial* growers of program crops but concentrated by acreage.

**(d) WTO box.** Mixed and contested. US **Amber Box / OTDS ≈ $17.7 billion** (2015–17 avg, 2020 dollars); **Green Box ≈ $127.5 billion**. *(Verified — [CRS R46811](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R46811.html).)* PLC and MALs are **Amber/price-linked** (product-specific AMS); ARC and crop insurance are argued as less-distorting but crop insurance premium subsidy counts largely as Amber. The US thus keeps a large *distorting* footprint but well under its bound ceiling.

**(e) What India could borrow.** The **CCC loan-rate / LDP mechanism is the highest-value, lowest-storage import**: a per-quintal floor that pays the *gap* to the farmer while the grain moves through private trade — exactly the "deficiency payment for the 70% you cannot store" that the [main report §4–5](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md) recommends for non-staples and perishables. India's MP Bhavantar/PDPS was a crude version of this and failed on price-discovery and gate-fraud (main report §3); the US design lessons are the *nonrecourse loan* framing (farmer keeps upside) and national-price repayment rates.

## 2. European Union (CAP) — the cautionary tale: built India's model, drowned in it, then exited to decoupled income support

**(a) Core instrument today.** **Decoupled area-based direct income payments** — the Basic Income Support for Sustainability (BISS), the descendant of the 2003 Single Farm Payment. Over **€185 billion of direct payments** across the 2023–27 CAP period, **~73% of Pillar I is decoupled**, and these are **notified in the WTO Green Box**. *(Verified — [EC income-support factsheet](https://agriculture.ec.europa.eu/common-agricultural-policy/income-support/income-support-explained_en); [CRS R46811](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R46811.html).)* Total CAP is **~€264 billion for CAP Strategic Plans 2023–27** within a ~€378.5 billion 2021–27 envelope. *(Verified — EC/JCEA.)*

**(b) How it differs — and why it's the key lesson for India.** The EU is the **historical proof of exactly what India is now doing**. Under the original CAP (1960s–80s), guaranteed minimum **intervention prices** prompted the EEC to **buy and store unlimited surplus** — the famous **"butter mountains," "milk lakes," and "grain mountains"**; butter stocks peaked at **1.283 million tonnes in 1986**. *(Verified — [Wikipedia/CAP Reform/EH.net](https://capreform.eu/return-of-the-butter-mountain/).)* This is India's 476.85-LMT stock pile in a different currency. The EU then **deliberately dismantled** it:

- **1992 MacSharry reform** — cut intervention prices, replaced guaranteed prices with **compensatory direct payments** when prices fell. *(Verified.)*
- **1999 Agenda 2000** — deepened price cuts, built the rural-development "second pillar." *(Verified.)*
- **2003 Fischler reform** — **full decoupling**: the Single Farm Payment paid **per eligible hectare regardless of what (or whether) you produce**, cutting the link between support and output. *(Verified — [EUR-Lex](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=LEGISSUM:l11089).)*

Intervention buying **still exists but only as a residual, deep safety net**: public intervention for common wheat is set at **€101.31/tonne for up to 3 million tonnes**, a price **unchanged since 2000/01** and now far below market — beyond that ceiling, purchases happen **only by tender at the Commission's discretion**. *(Verified — [CAP Reform](https://capreform.eu/intervention-arrangements-in-the-new-cap/); [EC market measures](https://agriculture.ec.europa.eu/common-agricultural-policy/market-measures/market-measures-explained_en).)* The EU kept the tool but **let its real value wither**, converting it from a price *target* into a rarely-touched floor.

**(c) Fiscal scale.** ~€53 billion/year total CAP; direct payments ~€37 billion/year. *(Verified — EC budget.)* Green Box outlays **~€73.5 billion**, Amber/OTDS **~€16.3 billion** (2015–17 avg). *(Verified — CRS R46811.)*

**(d) WTO box.** The decoupling was *engineered* to shift support **out of the Amber Box into the Green Box** — the whole point of area-based decoupled payments is that they are "notified under the WTO Green Box... no trade-distorting effects." *(Verified — [EC](https://agriculture.ec.europa.eu/common-agricultural-policy/income-support/income-support-explained_en).)*

**(e) What India could borrow.** Two things. First, the **negative lesson** the [main report §6 explicitly flagged as an open question](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md): open-ended intervention buying produces surplus mountains and fiscal traps; the EU's exit is the precedent for *not* universalising MSV. Second, the **transition template**: the EU did not abolish support, it **converted price support into decoupled per-hectare income payments** — the political-economy move most analogous to India replacing non-staple MSP with an enhanced, area-linked PM-KISAN (the ₹1.22 lakh crore option). The EU shows this is politically survivable if the cash transfer is large and visible.

## 3. China — the dual-track model closest to India's, which *also* hit the surplus wall and pivoted to deficiency payments

**(a) Core instrument.** A genuine **dual track**, structurally the nearest analogue to India:

- **Minimum purchase prices for rice and wheat** — an MSP-equivalent. State agent **Sinograin (China Grain Reserves Corp.)** buys when market price stays below the minimum for three consecutive days and **suspends when it rises above** for three days — a trigger-based version of open-ended procurement, but **capped by volume**: e.g. rice minimum-price purchases capped at **50 million tonnes** (20 Mt indica + 30 Mt japonica), wheat at **37 million tonnes** since 2019. *(Verified — [Frontiers 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2025.1536002/full); [Global Trade Alert 2024](https://globaltradealert.org/intervention/133734).)* Early-indica minimum price rose 1.20→1.28 yuan/jin (2019→2025). *(Reported — Frontiers/Baidu.)*
- **Reserves (Sinograin) are institutionally SEPARATED from the commercial arm (COFCO)** — the same separation the [agri-trader-playbook §3.1](agri-trader-playbook.md) credits for COFCO's success. The reserve does food security; COFCO trades.

**(b) How it differs — the 2014–16 pivot India should study.** China built temporary-reserve **stockpiling** for cotton, soybeans and corn and, like India, **drowned in it** — ruinous corn and cotton stocks by 2013–14. It then **pivoted to "target price" direct subsidies**:

- **Xinjiang cotton target-price pilot (2014–16)**: temporary reserve scrapped; farmers instead paid a **wedge subsidy = target price − average market price** (target 19,800 yuan/MT in 2014, cut to 19,100 then 18,600). *(Verified — [USDA-FAS/Choices Magazine](https://www.choicesmagazine.org/choices-magazine/theme-articles/us-commodity-markets-respond-to-changes-in-chinas-ag-policies/chinas-evolving-agricultural-support-policies).)*
- **Northeast soybean target-price pilot (2014)** — same year, same logic. *(Verified.)*
- **Corn price support abolished March 2016** — NDRC ended it explicitly because of "excessive stocks of corn, environmental concerns, unsustainable farming." *(Verified — Choices/USDA-FAS.)*

So China ran India's exact experiment — administered price + state stockpile — hit the same wall (surplus, storage, fiscal cost), and **converted three crops from procurement to deficiency payments while keeping procurement only for the two core staples (rice, wheat)**. That is almost identically the [main report §5 recommendation](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md): retain rice-wheat procurement, shift the rest to income/deficiency support.

**(c) Fiscal scale.** China raised its **2025 grain-stockpiling budget**; total grain purchases ~415 Mt in 2025, ~22.5 Mt bought under minimum-price schemes in 2025. *(Reported — [gov.cn](https://english.www.gov.cn/archive/statistics/202512/31/content_WS695475f3c6d00ca5f9a08586.html); [CNBC 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/03/05/china-raises-2025-budget-for-grain-stockpiling-targets-higher-domestic-output).)* (China does not publish a clean single farm-support total comparable to the Farm Bill.)

**(d) WTO box.** Minimum purchase prices = **Amber Box (product-specific AMS)**; China lost the 2019 WTO dispute (DS511) over rice and wheat AMS exceeding its de-minimis. Target-price/wedge subsidies were partly designed to reduce that exposure (though they can still be Amber). *(Reported — USDA-FAS; DS511 is verified WTO record but not re-fetched here, so labelled reported.)*

**(e) What India could borrow.** China is the **single most transferable case** because its starting point (dual-track, staple procurement + state reserve separated from trader, WTO-AMS-constrained, huge small-farmer base) is India's starting point. The borrowable move: **cap procurement by volume per crop** (China's 50 Mt rice / 37 Mt wheat caps are the analogue of the main report's "capped, pre-registered procurement volumes"), and **convert non-staples crop-by-crop to target-price wedge payments** rather than attempting universal purchase. China proves the pivot is administratively doable within a state-reserve system.

## 4. Brazil — the market-based price-floor model India rarely discusses (premiums + public put options)

**(a) Core instrument.** **PGPM** (Política de Garantia de Preços Mínimos) sets minimum guaranteed prices, but — crucially — **Brazil does NOT back them with unlimited government purchase**. CONAB (the food-supply agency, on behalf of the ag ministry MAPA) delivers the floor mostly through **market-based instruments**:

- **PEP (Prêmio para Escoamento de Produto)** — a **premium paid to private buyers/traders** to purchase the product from farmers at the minimum price and move it to deficit regions. The government pays the *gap*, private trade does the *handling*. *(Verified — [OECD Brazil 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/10/agricultural-policy-monitoring-and-evaluation-2025_354e7040/full-report/brazil_43e67f35.html); ResearchGate "Price Subsidy Policy in Brazilian Agriculture".)*
- **PEPRO (Prêmio Equalizador Pago ao Produtor)** — the same premium paid **directly to the producer** (an income deficiency payment = minimum reference price − market price). *(Verified — OECD Brazil 2025.)*
- **Public sell/put options (contratos de opção de venda)** — CONAB **auctions put options to farmers**: for a premium the farmer buys the *right* (not obligation) to sell to the government at a strike (the minimum price) at expiry. If market price is above strike at expiry the option lapses and the state buys nothing; if below, the farmer exercises and the state pays out. This is a **genuinely different, contingent, cheaper price floor** — the fiscal cost is only the premium plus any in-the-money exercise, not open-ended storage. Recent example: CONAB negotiated **~91,700 tonnes of rice via option-contract auctions (3,396 contracts, ~R$162.2 million allocated if exercised)**. *(Verified — [S&P Global 2024](https://www.spglobal.com/commodity-insights/en/news-research/latest-news/agriculture/122724-brazils-conab-auctions-91700-mt-of-rice).)*
- **AGF (Aquisições do Governo Federal)** — residual *direct* government purchase, used sparingly, not the primary tool. *(Reported — OECD.)*

**(b) How it differs from Indian MSP.** Brazil delivers a minimum-price *guarantee* while shifting **both the logistics and most of the fiscal risk off the government balance sheet**. The put-option auction is the cleanest contrast with India: instead of standing ready to buy everything at MSP (India) or paying a flat wedge on all output (China target price), Brazil sells farmers a **contingent claim** priced at auction — the state only pays when the floor actually binds, and never accumulates a permanent buffer. Coverage is region- and crop-selective, not universal.

**(c) Fiscal scale.** Deliberately small: the government allocated **~BRL 1 billion (~US$303 million) in 2024–25** to PEP/PEPRO combined (purchases + income-deficiency payments). *(Verified — [OECD Brazil 2025](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2025/10/agricultural-policy-monitoring-and-evaluation-2025_354e7040/full-report/brazil_43e67f35.html).)* Orders of magnitude below India's ₹2 lakh-crore food subsidy — because it is a floor mechanism, not a procurement-and-distribution system.

**(d) WTO box.** PGPM minimum prices and the premiums are **Amber Box (product-specific)**, but Brazil's total support is small relative to its bound ceiling, so this is not a binding constraint. *(Reported — OECD.)*

**(e) What India could borrow.** The **put-option auction is the most novel and cheapest import** and directly extends the [main report §5 architecture](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md). For non-staple, storable crops where India cannot afford open-ended procurement, CONAB-style **public put options auctioned to pre-registered farmers** would (i) give a *credible* floor, (ii) let private trade do the movement (the PEP premium-to-mover idea maps onto the main report's "contract the offtake before the purchase — weekly e-auctions restricted to processors"), and (iii) cap fiscal cost at the option premium plus in-the-money exercise. This is the market-based delivery of a floor that India's Bhavantar tried and botched.

## 5. Indonesia (BULOG) and Egypt (GASC/Mostakbal Misr) — state logistics-and-reserve agencies WITHOUT an open-ended price guarantee

**(a) Core instruments.**

- **Indonesia — Perum BULOG.** A state enterprise managing the **public rice reserve (CBP)** and **market operations (SPHP)**. It buys domestic rice at a **Government Purchase Price (HPP, a floor)** and defends a **retail ceiling (HET)** by releasing reserve rice into the market when prices spike. But BULOG is a **commercial entity acting as a Public Service Obligation arm** — it "can only play its role effectively if market conditions meet its parameters," i.e. it does **not** guarantee to buy all output at HPP the way FCI does. Reserve distribution: **2.76 Mt (2023) → 3.37 Mt (2024)**; SPHP retail-price band **12,500–15,800 IDR/kg (2024/25)**. *(Verified — [FFTC-AP](https://ap.fftc.org.tw/article/1281); [ANTARA](https://en.antaranews.com/news/419863/indonesia-distributes-77-mln-tons-of-rice-reserves-in-2023-2025); [Jakarta Post 2023](https://www.thejakartapost.com/paper/2023/04/04/reform-bulog-not-rice-imports.html).)*
- **Egypt — GASC (now Mostakbal Misr).** GASC was the state **monopsony wheat importer** for the subsidised-bread (baladi) system feeding ~70 million people; it **procures ~3.5 Mt domestically (~$1 billion) and imports ~5.5 Mt via international tenders**, holding strategic reserves. In **December 2024** the military's **Mostakbal Misr agency took over** as exclusive strategic-commodity importer, and in mid-2024 Egypt raised the subsidised-bread price for the **first time in 36 years**. *(Verified — [Reuters/US News 2024](https://www.usnews.com/news/world/articles/2024-12-06/egypts-mostakbal-misr-takes-over-gascs-role-as-commodities-importer); [USDA-FAS GAIN EG2024-0013](https://www.fas.usda.gov/data/egypt-egypt-increases-price-subsidized-bread-first-time-36-years).)*

**(b) How they differ from Indian MSP.** Both are **consumer-side / supply-security** agencies, not farmer-price-guarantee agencies. BULOG's HPP is a *thin* floor subordinate to its market-stabilisation and PSO role; it is **not an open-ended purchase promise**. Egypt's model is even more consumption-driven: the state is a **buyer for a bread subsidy**, and its domestic procurement is a minor share (3.5 of 9 Mt) with the rest **imported** — the opposite of India's self-procurement. Neither runs India's price-support-by-unlimited-purchase logic.

**(c) Fiscal scale / coverage.** Egypt: domestic wheat procurement ~$1 billion + import bill; the bread subsidy is the dominant fiscal item (the reason the 36-year-frozen price finally rose). BULOG: reserve of ~3 Mt distributed annually; fiscal cost embedded in the PSO budget. *(Reported.)*

**(d) WTO box.** Consumer food subsidies and public stockholding are largely **Green Box (general services / domestic food aid)** or, for public stockholding at administered prices, the same Amber/peace-clause zone India occupies — but neither Indonesia nor Egypt is a headline AMS-breach case. *(Unverified — my classification, not separately sourced.)*

**(e) What India could borrow.** The **"logistics-and-reserve-only" model** is the *floor* of ambition — a state that guarantees **supply and a ceiling for consumers** and only a *thin, discretionary* floor for farmers. For India this is relevant as the design for **buffer operations decoupled from open-ended price support**: run FCI/CWC as a BULOG-style stabiliser (buy some, release to cap spikes) for crops where a *price guarantee* is unaffordable, rather than promising MSP purchase. Egypt is the cautionary note that a consumer-subsidy monopsony becomes fiscally frozen and politically un-reformable (36 years).

## 6. Japan and South Korea — the "protect + divert + pay" model (border walls + production adjustment + direct payments)

**(a) Core instruments.**

- **Japan.** Historically the **gentan** rice production-adjustment (acreage-reduction) scheme paid farmers to *not* grow rice, propping the price by restricting supply. The direct **per-10a payment (7,500 yen/10a) and income-support direct payments were halved in 2014 and abolished in 2018**, and the formal gentan quota system was "lifted" in 2018 — though the state **still subsidises farmers who divert rice land to other crops (soybeans, feed)**. *(Verified — [FFTC-AP](https://ap.fftc.org.tw/article/1329); [USDA-FAS](https://www.fas.usda.gov/data/japan-maff-2018-budget-proposal-shifts-spending-income-insurance); Canon Institute.)* Behind this sits a very high rice tariff wall.
- **South Korea.** **Tariffication in 2015**: a **513% out-of-quota rice tariff** above a 408,700 MT minimum-access quota, replacing rigid import quotas. Domestically, a **target-price system with fixed + variable direct payments** compensated part of farm-income loss. Korea's base **AMS was 1,718.6 billion won (~US$1.43 billion) in 1994, >91% from rice**. *(Verified — [Korea Herald](https://www.koreaherald.com/article/2543656); ECIPE; USDA-FAS.)*

**(b) How they differ from Indian MSP.** The primary defence is the **border (tariffs), not domestic procurement**. High tariffs keep the *internal* price high without the state having to buy the crop; the domestic instruments then **manage surplus by paying farmers to divert/reduce acreage** (supply control) plus **decoupled/target-price direct payments** for income. India, bound by lower agricultural tariffs on many crops and committed to consumer-price restraint, cannot lean on a 513% wall; and India *adds* supply (procurement) rather than *restricting* it (set-aside). This is the mirror image of India's problem.

**(c) Fiscal scale / coverage.** Japan shifted budget from price/acreage support toward **income insurance (収入保険)** in the 2018 reform. *(Reported — USDA-FAS 2018.)* Korea's rice direct payments run in the low-trillions of won. *(Reported.)*

**(d) WTO box.** Both faced **AMS pressure** driving reform: Korea's tariffication and the fixed/variable payment split were partly designed to move support toward **Blue Box (production-limiting)** and **Green Box (decoupled fixed)** and away from Amber. Japan's diversion payments are production-limiting (Blue-Box-flavoured). *(Reported.)*

**(e) What India could borrow.** The transferable piece is **production adjustment / diversion payments** — paying farmers to shift rice-wheat land to pulses, oilseeds, millets. This directly serves the [main report's](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md) crop-diversification aim (breaking the Punjab rice-wheat lock-in) and the millet-mandate idea, and is **Blue-Box eligible** (production-limiting), giving WTO headroom India lacks in the Amber Box. India cannot copy the tariff wall, but it can copy the **divert-and-pay** half.

## 7. Brief cautions — Turkey (TMO) and Zambia/Malawi (Food Reserve Agency + FISP)

**(a) Turkey — TMO (Toprak Mahsulleri Ofisi).** A state grain board that **announces annual purchase prices** for wheat/barley/etc. and **buys into strategic reserves**, then **releases stock to curb inflation** (e.g. ~3.9 Mt of grain released Feb 2026). 2026 wheat purchase price 16,500 TL/tonne; TMO silo prices ~$320/tonne, a $15–20/t discount to open market. *(Verified/Reported — [Global Trade Alert](https://www.globaltradealert.org/intervention/137215/price-stabilisation/turkiye-turkish-grain-board-announces-the-2024-purchase-prices-for-cereals-including-wheat-and-barley); [Milling MEA 2026](https://millingmea.com/turkey-mobilises-nearly-4mmt-of-grain-stocks-to-curb-inflation-support-pasta-and-feed-sectors/).)* Structurally the **closest to India** among the small cases — administered price + buffer — and shows the same tension: the board becomes an inflation-management tool as much as a farmer-support tool, and its prices are whipsawed by macro (Turkish lira) instability. *(Analysis — unverified synthesis.)*

**(b) Zambia (FRA) and Malawi — the documented failure cases.** The **Food Reserve Agency (FRA)** buys maize at a **pan-territorial floor price**; the **Farmer Input Support Programme (FISP)** subsidises fertiliser/seed. Both are heavily documented **failures India should treat as red flags**:

- **Fiscal trap:** the FRA floor price and input subsidy consume the bulk of the agriculture budget, with expenditure routinely exceeding plan — a "deepening fiscal trap." *(Verified — [World Bank 2022](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099845009122237020/pdf/P1770680cbdc81030a9e4092dc8bf6311f.pdf); Zambian Observer 2026.)*
- **Crowding out:** the subsidised FRA floor "crowded out" commercial producers, millers and traders who "cannot compete with the government's highly subsidised floor price," and **biased production toward maize monoculture**, discouraging diversification. *(Verified — [ResearchGate/World Bank](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308644983_Zambia_Food_Reserve_Agency_Pricing_Mechanisms_and_the_Impact_on_Maize_Markets).)*
- **Leakage / poor targeting:** FISP fertiliser benefit-cost ratios **well below 1**; ~30–35% of the time inputs did not reach intended beneficiaries. *(Verified — ResearchGate/IMF.)*

**(c) What India could borrow — mostly what to AVOID.** Zambia/Malawi are the **small-country version of India's own risks**: a single-crop administered floor (maize ≈ India's rice-wheat lock-in) that crowds out private trade, entrenches monoculture, and turns into an un-exitable fiscal drain. They validate two main-report cautions: **cap the volume** (Zambia's uncapped floor is the anti-pattern, exactly like Haryana's loss-making uncapped bajra buying, [main report §5.1](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md)) and **do not let the floor displace private markets**.

### 8.2 The taxonomy — five distinct models

1. **Physical-procurement-and-reserve** *(India · China pre-2014 · Turkey · Zambia/Malawi)* — administered price backed by government purchase into a public buffer. **The model India runs.** Its universal failure mode is documented on four continents: surplus accumulation, storage physics as the binding constraint, fiscal drain, private-market crowding-out, monoculture lock-in. Every large economy that ran it (EU, China) has partially or wholly exited.

2. **Decoupled income support** *(EU · US direct payments pre-2014)* — per-hectare/per-farm cash **detached from what or whether you produce**, Green Box, no procurement. The "drowned-then-exited" destination. Politically survivable only because the cash is large and visible.

3. **Deficiency-payment / counter-cyclical** *(US PLC-ARC + CCC loan/LDP · China post-2014 target price · Brazil PEPRO · Korea variable payment)* — the government pays the **gap between a reference/target price and the market price**, in cash, while the crop stays in commercial trade. The destination India actually debates (IEG WP420's "deficiency payment on the 70% you can't store").

4. **Market-based price-floor** *(Brazil put options + PEP premiums-to-movers)* — the floor is delivered by **auctioned contingent instruments** (public put options) and **premiums paid to private buyers to move the grain**. Cheapest and most surgical; fiscal cost capped at premium + in-the-money exercise; the state never accumulates a buffer. **The model India least discusses and arguably should discuss most.**

5. **State-logistics-reserve-only** *(BULOG · GASC/Egypt)* — the state guarantees **consumer supply and a price ceiling**, holds a strategic reserve, and offers farmers only a **thin, discretionary floor** — no open-ended price guarantee. The minimum-ambition option: buffer operations decoupled from price support.

## 9. Mapping India's realistic reform path onto the models

India's constraints are specific and political-economy-binding (all consistent with the [main report](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md)):

- **Cannot abolish MSP** (political third rail; farmer-union demand is *legal* MSP, i.e. *more* guarantee, not less).
- **Huge small-farmer base**, most of whom never sell to procurement (NSS SAS: 8.8% of agri households sold to agencies) — so a *pure* decoupled per-hectare payment (EU model) reaches them better than procurement does, but land-record gaps make hectare-based payment leaky.
- **WTO peace-clause exposure** on rice (five invocations, Amber-Box de-minimis breached) — so *adding* Amber-Box procurement (universal MSV) worsens the exposure; *shifting* to Green/Blue reduces it.
- **Storage physics** caps procurement at ~30% of the ~261 Mt marketed surplus of 14 crops ([main report §4](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md)) — a universal purchase guarantee fails on warehouses before money.

**The fit.** No single foreign model transplants. But the **China + Brazil + Blue-Box-diversion composite is the realistic path**, and it *is* the [main report §5 hybrid MSV](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md) restated in comparative terms:

1. **Keep physical procurement for rice and wheat, volume-capped** — the **China model** (50 Mt rice / 37 Mt wheat caps), matching the main report's "capped, pre-registered procurement volumes per crop." Do **not** universalise it (the EU/Zambia lesson).
2. **Extend a volume floor to pulses** where NAFED is relatively efficient and 100% tur/urad/masur commitments already exist ([main report §3, §5](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md)) — a crop-limited **physical-procurement** extension, but only where storage and processing offtake exist.
3. **Shift non-staples to deficiency payments** — the **US PLC/LDP + China target-price** model. Pay the price gap in cash; let private trade move the grain. This is the ₹1.22 lakh crore enhanced-PM-KISAN direction, ~⅓ the ₹3.40 lakh crore universal-MSP cost, and it **moves support toward the Green/Amber-de-minimis-safe zone**, easing the peace-clause problem that universal MSV would inflame.
4. **Pilot Brazil-style public put options** for one or two storable non-staples (a pulse, maize) — a **market-based price-floor** that gives a credible guarantee at capped fiscal cost, and pairs naturally with the main report's "contract the offtake before the purchase" (PEP premium-to-processor logic).
5. **Add Blue-Box diversion payments** (Japan/Korea "protect-divert-pay" half, minus the tariff wall India can't build) to move Punjab rice-wheat land toward pulses/oilseeds/millets — production-limiting, WTO-favourable, and serving diversification.

**The one-line synthesis.** *India is at the point the EU reached in 1986 and China reached in 2013 — a growing surplus mountain from an open-ended administered-price-plus-procurement system. Both exited: the EU to decoupled income payments, China to volume caps plus target-price deficiency payments. India's political economy rules out the EU's clean decoupling and the Japan/Korea tariff wall, but the **China volume-cap + US/Brazil deficiency-and-option composite** is available, cheaper, and less WTO-exposed than the universal MSV that farmer unions demand — which is exactly why the [main report's hybrid](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md) (cap procurement, deficiency-pay the rest, contract offtake first) is the internationally-validated answer.*
