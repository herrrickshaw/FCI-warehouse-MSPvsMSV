# Rice-mill business model: milling, husk, and RBO extraction economics

*Grounded in a real cooperative rice-mill DPR (NCDC/CSISAC, 1 TPH, Chhattisgarh —
user-supplied) that replaces the estimated milling constants used elsewhere with an actual
project cost sheet. Cross-refs [biodiesel-blending-scenarios.md](biodiesel-blending-scenarios.md)
and [omss-processing-returns.md](omss-processing-returns.md).*

## Why this DPR matters: it makes the project's argument, officially

The DPR's own thesis is the project's thesis, from a government cooperative-development
document: **the cooperative societies are *already* the paddy-procurement centres earning a
₹30/qtl commission — so the value-add step is to MILL what they already procure**, capturing
the byproduct stream instead of handing paddy to private millers. "The Rice milling could be
the turnaround activity for the cooperative societies… their income can be increased by
adding the milling activity." That is exactly the "process, don't sell raw" case — here
endorsed in an NCDC feasibility report.

## The real mass balance (verified from DPR)

| Fraction | DPR % | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Rice | 67–69% | net realisation taken at **67%** (matches FCI CMR outturn) |
| Broken rice | 12% | sold separately @ ₹1,500/qtl |
| Bran | **10%** | @ ₹2,000/qtl to oil extractors; **20–22% oil content** |
| Husk | 6% *(saleable)* | @ ₹2,000/MT — see nuance below |
| Wastage | 5% | |

**The husk nuance (important correction):** the DPR books husk at only **6%** — but husk is
physically ~20% of paddy. The gap is because **most husk is burned captively as mill fuel**
("used as fuel in power tanks, brick industries and rice mill"); only ~6% is *sold*. This
directly confirms the blending model's framing: **husk is already an energy input, not a
saleable commodity** — it powers the mill, and only the surplus reaches the market. Husk's
role is process energy, exactly as modelled.

## Milling economics (verified — 1 TPH cooperative mill)

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Total project cost | **₹80 lakh** (machinery ₹55L + civil ₹5L + WC ₹15.6L + pre-op ₹2L) |
| Term loan / subsidy / society | ₹52L / ₹16L (CSISAC) / ₹12L |
| Custom-milling commission (govt) | **₹35/qtl** |
| Paddy-procurement commission | ₹30/qtl |
| Own paddy in / rice out | ₹1,800/qtl paddy → ₹3,300/qtl rice |
| **IRR** | **19%** (norm >15%) |
| Avg DSCR | 2.56 (norm >2%) |
| Payback | 5 yr 2 mo |
| Profit on sales | 8.5% → 15.4% (yrs II→I; ramps with utilisation) |
| Break-even capacity | 63–80% |

This grounds the OMSS-returns model's ~22% ROCE estimate: a real cooperative mill earns
**19% IRR / 8.5–15% profit-on-sales** — the mid-single-to-low-double-digit processing return
the three-tier margin ladder predicted, *below* branded staples (KRBL/LT Foods 11–12% EBITDA)
and *above* raw trading (2–4%).

## The byproduct prices that ground the RBO cost model

The DPR gives real transacted prices (Chhattisgarh, ~2018 vintage):

- **Husk: ₹2,000/MT = ₹0.20/kg** — fuel-grade, near-worthless per kg (its value is the
  energy it displaces, not the sale).
- **Bran: ₹2,000/quintal = ₹20/kg**, at **20–22% oil** — this is the RBO feedstock cost.
- **Broken rice: ₹1,500/qtl = ₹15/kg** — the ethanol/rice-flour feedstock.

**Reconciling RBO extraction cost** (the number the blending model needs): naïvely, bran at
₹20/kg ÷ 20% oil = ₹100/kg of crude oil — *at* import parity, seemingly uneconomic. But the
**de-oiled rice bran (DORB)** left after extraction is sold as feed at ~₹15/kg:

```
1 kg bran (₹20) → 0.20 kg crude RBO + 0.78 kg DORB (feed @ ₹15 = ₹11.70 credit)
net bran cost of the oil = (20 − 11.70) / 0.20 = ₹41.5/kg crude RBO
+ solvent extraction + refining (~₹15–20/kg)  →  ~₹57–62/kg ex-mill RBO
vs import parity ₹100–110/kg  →  a ₹40–50/kg margin
```

**The DORB feed credit is what makes rice bran oil cheap** — it converts a ₹100/kg-looking
feedstock into ~₹60/kg oil, confirming the agent's ₹55–75/kg ex-mill figure and the
~₹8,200 cr import-substitution prize. Without counting DORB, RBO looks uneconomic; with it,
it beats imports by ₹40–50/kg.

## What the DPR clarifies for the integrated thesis

1. **Milling is a real ~19% IRR business** — not a giveaway — which is *why* private millers
   fight for CMR paddy allotments (documented in the Haryana brief) and *why* the government
   gifting them the bran (₹7,650 cr, [omss-processing-returns.md](omss-processing-returns.md))
   is leaving genuine value on the table.
2. **Husk is captive energy, confirmed** — ~6% saleable of ~20% gross; the rest fuels the
   mill. The integrated biorefinery's "husk powers the process" assumption is standard mill
   practice, not an aspiration.
3. **Bran economics hinge on the DORB credit** — any RBO import-substitution policy must keep
   the de-oiled bran in the feed chain (not divert it), or the oil cost inverts.
4. **The cooperative-mill route is the delivery vehicle** — societies that already procure
   paddy at MSP adding a ₹80-lakh 1-TPH mill is a proven, financeable (CSISAC-subsidised,
   19% IRR) model for capturing the byproduct cascade at source — the practical answer to
   "how does the state process rather than sell raw."

## Caveats

DPR vintage ~2018, Chhattisgarh cooperative context; prices (bran ₹20/kg, husk ₹2,000/MT)
are that vintage and region — current bran prices are higher (the 2022 "rice bran becomes hot
commodity" reporting). The husk 6% is *saleable* not gross (~20% physical); the DPR's mass
balance lists broken rice separately from the 67% rice fraction. IRR/DSCR are the DPR's own
projections under its stated assumptions (10% annual price escalation, 75–90% utilisation),
not audited outcomes. A 1 TPH mill is small; economics improve with scale.
