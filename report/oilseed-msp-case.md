# The case for effective MSP procurement of groundnut & mustard under NMEO

*An honest advocacy case: why assured procurement of the two big indigenous oilseeds is the
missing lever in the edible-oil self-sufficiency mission — and what it can and cannot do.
Grounded in the project's verified parity, procurement, and consumption findings. July 2026.*

## First, the two missions (a needed clarification)

"NMEO-OP" is the **National Mission on Edible Oils – Oil Palm** — plantation crop, NE and
Andhra/Telangana, a long-gestation tree crop. **Groundnut and mustard fall under its sibling,
NMEO-Oilseeds (NMEO-OS)** — ₹10,103 cr, 2024-25 to 2030-31, targeting oilseed output
**39 → 69.7 MT by 2030-31**. The case below is for NMEO-OS's two workhorses; together the two
missions are India's bid to cut a **56% edible-oil import dependence** that cost **~₹80,000 cr
in crude palm oil alone in FY26** (imports 82 LMT, rising).

## The honest framing: what procurement can and cannot do

Be clear up front, because overclaiming discredits the case:

- **It will NOT cut oil imports this year.** Mustard (~115 LMT) is *already* almost entirely
  crushed into oil — India's largest indigenous edible oil (~42 LMT). Procuring it changes
  *who* crushes the same seed, adding ~zero new oil in-year. Groundnut is ~25–30% crushed
  (much goes to food/seed/export). Procurement is a **near-zero in-year lever on the oil
  deficit** ([oilseeds-biodiesel-consumption.md](oilseeds-biodiesel-consumption.md), Q1).
- **It will NOT make cooking oil cheaper for the mass consumer.** Domestic mustard oil runs
  ~₹136/kg ex-mill vs imported palm ~₹98–120/kg. Mustard/groundnut are *premium, preference-led*
  oils (North/East mustard, West/South groundnut), not palm substitutes. Procurement supports
  the premium tier, not the price tier.

So why do it? Because **the real lever on the oil deficit is production growth, and assured
procurement is the one thing that de-risks the farmer's decision to grow more oilseed.** That
is the case — and it is strong.

## The core argument: procurement is the production-growth catalyst NMEO needs

NMEO-OS lives or dies on getting oilseed output from 39 to 69.7 MT. That means farmers
choosing oilseeds over the alternative — and the alternative (paddy, wheat) carries an
**assured MSP that binds**, while oilseeds carry an MSP that mostly doesn't:

| | Assured procurement | Price risk faced by farmer |
|---|---|---|
| Paddy / wheat | 23–37% of output bought at MSP, reliably | low — the floor is real |
| Mustard / groundnut | **8–12% in good years, ~0 in others** (repo data) | **high — the floor is a notification** |

A farmer weighing mustard against wheat sees the same MSP promise but a *credible* one for
wheat and a *hollow* one for oilseeds. **That asymmetry is why oilseed acreage stalls and
import dependence deepens.** Making the mustard/groundnut floor bind — a capped, pre-announced
MSV (θ ≈ 0.10–0.15) that NAFED/NCCF actually execute — removes the price risk that keeps
farmers out of oilseeds. Production growth is what adds oil; assured procurement is what
unlocks production growth. Procurement is not the oil lever directly — it is the lever on the
lever.

## The parity trap procurement breaks

Without a binding floor, oilseeds are caught in a self-reinforcing import trap:

1. Cheap palm/soy imports undercut the domestic oilseed price (palm landed ~₹98/kg vs mustard
   MSP-implied ~₹136/kg oil).
2. Domestic oilseed prices fall below MSP at harvest; farmers take the loss.
3. Farmers exit oilseeds for the crop with the real floor (paddy/wheat).
4. Domestic oil output shrinks; import dependence rises; repeat.

**Assured procurement + the tariff lever together break the loop** — the "procure-process-
protect" package from the [diversification strategy](product-diversification-strategy.md).
The tariff is already moving (crude palm BCD raised to 20% in Sep-2024, the crude-refined gap
collapsing refined palmolein imports from 21 to 3.4 LMT) — proof the state can defend the
floor. Procurement without the tariff bleeds; procurement *with* it is a viable production
incentive. They are one instrument.

## The double dividend, and the byproduct that pays

Procuring and crushing mustard/groundnut delivers two returns rice/wheat procurement never
can, plus a byproduct that offsets the cost:

- **Farmer income** — a real floor for ~2 crore oilseed growers, the NMEO-OS constituency.
- **Import substitution** — every incremental LMT of domestic oil (via production growth)
  displaces palm at ~₹80,000-cr-a-year scale; forex saved, not just subsidy spent.
- **Oilmeal byproduct** — mustard/groundnut crush yields ~55–60% de-oiled cake, a high-protein
  feed and export earner (India exports oilmeals) that offsets the crush cost — the same
  byproduct-cascade logic that makes rice-bran oil cheap ([rice-mill-business-model.md](rice-mill-business-model.md)).

## The design (so it binds, and doesn't repeat the bajra mistake)

1. **Capped, pre-announced MSV volume** (θ ≈ 0.10–0.15 of output) per crop, per state, sized
   to what NAFED/NCCF can actually crush or store — not the uncapped commitment that turned
   bajra procurement into loss-making resale.
2. **Crush, don't stockpile.** Oilseeds degrade; route procured seed to crushing (HAFED's
   own-account oil-mill model), keep the oil in the domestic pool, sell the meal. Buffer the
   oil briefly, not the seed.
3. **Pair with the tariff, always.** Announce the procurement floor and the import duty
   together; the floor is only credible behind the duty.
4. **Target the premium tier honestly.** Brand the mustard/groundnut oil (the preference-led
   segment will pay) — don't pretend it competes with palm; capture the margin where the
   consumer already prefers the product.
5. **Measure by acreage and output, not tonnes procured.** The success metric is the 39→69.7 MT
   production path, with procurement as the de-risking input — not procurement volume for its
   own sake.

## Bottom line

Adding groundnut and mustard to *effective, binding* MSP procurement will not cut oil imports
next year and will not cheapen palm — and any case that claims it will is wrong. What it will
do is remove the price risk that keeps farmers out of oilseeds, making it the missing
production-growth catalyst NMEO-OS needs to have any chance at its 39→69.7 MT target. Paired
with the tariff and the oilmeal byproduct credit, it is a farmer-income and long-run
import-substitution instrument — the lever on the lever that turns the edible-oil mission from
a subsidy into a self-sufficiency strategy. That is the honest, and sufficient, case.

## Caveats

Production-growth response to assured procurement is a directional argument, not a quantified
elasticity (no clean Indian estimate of oilseed acreage response to procurement credibility
was verified). Mustard "already fully crushed" and the ₹136 vs ₹98/kg spread are the project's
verified figures; MSPs (mustard ₹5,950, groundnut ₹7,263, 2025-26) are PIB-verified. The
fiscal cost of a binding θ for two crops is not modelled here — it belongs in the hybrid-MSV
cost frame ([msp-vs-msv-main-report.md](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md) §4). Palm import figures are
DGCI&S FY26 provisional.
