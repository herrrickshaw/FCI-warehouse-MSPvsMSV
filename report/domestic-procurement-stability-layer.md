# The case for buffering what we grow *and* import: a deficit-crop stability layer

*Why India should aggregate and buffer the crops it cultivates but does not procure —
oilseeds, pulses, and secondary sources like rice bran — to offset imports and stabilise
prices. Synthesis of the project's parity, byproduct, import-arm and diversification
findings. July 2026.*

## The paradox: India imports what it grows

India runs a procurement machine of extraordinary scale — for the two crops it does *not*
need to import. Rice and wheat are procured at 37% and 23% of output, piled 2× over buffer
norms, and disposed below cost. Meanwhile the crops India *does* import, in bulk, are the
ones the state barely aggregates at all:

- **Edible oil**: India is the **world's #1 producer of rice bran oil** and a top producer
  of groundnut, mustard, sesame — yet domestic output meets only **44%** of demand;
  **56.25% is imported** (2023-24), a ~₹19.5 bn/yr bill. *(NITI Aayog Aug 2024; NMEO PIB.)*
- **Pulses**: India is the world's largest pulse producer *and* largest importer —
  **6.7 MT imported in FY25 ($3.74 bn FY24)**, tur/urad duty-free to Mar-2026.

The state grows the crop and imports the crop. What it does *not* do is **aggregate the
domestic crop into a buffer** the way it does for rice and wheat. That missing layer is the
whole opportunity.

## Why the gap exists: procurement follows the surplus, not the deficit

The historical accident (see [pricing essay §2](pricing-msp-msv-published.html)): India
built its aggregation machine around the *surplus* cereals because that is where the PDS
mandate and the 1960s food-crisis politics pointed. The *deficit* crops — oilseeds, pulses —
were left to fragmented private trade and imports. So:

- MSP for oilseeds/pulses is announced but rarely binds (procurement 0–14% of output —
  the [production-vs-procurement data](../data/production_vs_procurement.csv)).
- Domestic oilseed and pulse output is under-collected, un-buffered, and price-volatile.
- Imports fill the gap — and set the price *below* MSP, so procurement looks uneconomic,
  so the state doesn't procure, so the gap persists. A self-reinforcing trap.

The deficit crops are stuck in the mirror image of the cereal problem: rice/wheat have
buffers but no import to offset; oilseeds/pulses have imports but no buffer to offset them.

## The rice-bran case: a domestic edible-oil source given away for free

Rice bran is the sharpest single illustration. India mills ~130 MT of paddy a year; the bran
byproduct is a **domestic edible-oil feedstock** — and India already leads the world in rice
bran oil. Yet:

- Under CMR, that bran is **handed to millers for free** — the ~₹7,650 cr/yr byproduct
  giveaway ([omss-processing-returns.md](omss-processing-returns.md), Finding 3).
- NMEO-Oilseeds *itself* names "increasing collection and extraction efficiency from
  secondary sources like **cottonseed, coconut, rice bran**" as a pillar — the policy admits
  the source is under-tapped. *(NMEO PIB, Dec 2025.)*

So India imports edible oil while giving away, and under-extracting, a domestic edible-oil
source it is already the world leader in. **Aggregating rice bran (and cottonseed) is
import substitution that requires no new acreage, no new water, no new MSP outlay — only
collection and crushing.** It is the lowest-hanging fruit in the entire edible-oil deficit.

## Why a deficit-crop buffer is economically superior to the cereal buffer

The decisive argument: a buffer in a crop you *import* pays for itself in ways the rice/wheat
buffer never can. The cereal buffer is pure negative carry (−8 to −15%/yr,
[holding-economics.md](holding-economics.md)) with no offsetting import to avoid. A
deficit-crop buffer's carry cost is offset by four returns the cereal buffer lacks:

| | Rice/wheat buffer | Oilseed/pulse/bran buffer |
|---|---|---|
| Carry cost | negative, unoffset | negative, but offset by ↓ |
| Import bill avoided | none (net exporter) | **the whole point — forex saved per tonne** |
| Tariff revenue | n/a | the duty the state sets *and collects* on the residual import |
| Byproduct | given away | oilmeal (feed), de-oiled bran — a revenue stream |
| Price-stability value | domestic only | kills the *import-price* spike that drives food inflation |

Every tonne of domestically-procured, buffered oilseed or pulse is a tonne of import
*not* bought — so the buffer's "cost" is netted against ~₹19.5 bn oil + $3.74 bn pulse
imports. This is the import-substitution dividend rice and wheat structurally cannot offer.

## The stability-layer design

A deficit-crop buffer does **double duty** — a farmer price floor *and* a consumer
price-stabilisation reserve — which is exactly the combination India's cereal buffer
achieves for the PDS and the deficit crops currently lack:

1. **Aggregate what we grow.** Extend capped MSV procurement (per the
   [hybrid design](msp-vs-msv-main-report.md), θ ≈ 0.15–0.30) to oilseeds and pulses — a
   pre-announced volume floor that finally makes MSP bind for the deficit crops, sized to
   the import bill it displaces, not to PDS need.
2. **Aggregate the secondary sources.** Collect and crush rice bran and cottonseed instead
   of gifting them — the free import-substitution layer NMEO already targets. Auction CMR
   with the bran priced in (recover the ₹7,650 cr) and direct it to bran-oil extraction.
3. **Buffer and release counter-cyclically.** Hold the domestic buffer; release it into the
   deficit-season price spike so the import is never needed at the peak — the classic
   buffer function, but on the crops where it actually saves forex and cools inflation.
4. **Protect the floor with the tariff.** Procure-process-protect as one package: raise the
   duty (tur → yellow-peas 30% model) when releasing the buffer, so imports don't cap the
   upside and crush the domestic procurement economics.
5. **Import the residual — hedged.** The [import arm](import-arm-evaluation.md) tops up the
   remaining gap on international exchanges (Bursa/CBOT/CME South Asia CPO), hedged, by open
   tender — while NMEO closes the acreage gap and the buffer shrinks the import need
   structurally.

The five pieces form one loop: **procure domestic → aggregate the byproduct → buffer →
release counter-cyclically → import only the hedged residual → substitute over time.** It is
the cereal buffer's machinery, redirected at the crops where the buffer earns its keep.

## The bottom line

India's procurement is aimed at the wrong crops. It buffers the surplus it can't sell and
leaves the deficit it must import un-buffered. Building a stability layer for the
cultivated-but-imported crops — oilseeds, pulses, and the rice bran it currently gives away —
turns procurement from a fiscal sink (cereals) into an import-substitution and
price-stabilisation engine (deficit crops), self-financed by the import bill it avoids, the
tariff it collects, and the byproduct it stops wasting. The state is already the world's #1
rice-bran-oil producer; it should stop importing the oil it is best in the world at making
from a byproduct it throws away.

## Caveats

The import-substitution "return" holds only behind a managed tariff (remove the duty and
domestic procurement inverts — tur FY26 is the live example). Rice-bran aggregation assumes
extraction and de-oiled-bran offtake capacity scales with collection. Oilseed/pulse buffer
volumes must respect storage physics (the ~777 LMT ceiling is mostly cereal-committed —
deficit-crop buffers need dedicated capacity, likely the silo/warehouse expansion). NMEO's
39→69.7 MT oilseed target (2030-31) is a stated goal, not an achieved outcome. Self-
sufficiency figures are NITI Aayog / NMEO vintage 2024-25.
