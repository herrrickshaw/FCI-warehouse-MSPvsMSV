# New and Strategic Markets for India's Grain Surplus: A Forward-Looking Evaluation

*Companion to the MSP-vs-MSV project, and the prospective counterpart to the historical record in [rice-diplomacy-history.md](./rice-diplomacy-history.md). Where that piece documented what India **has done** with grain as statecraft, this one evaluates where the surplus **can go next** — as humanitarian instrument, as commercial export, or as strategic lever. Written 2026-07-24. Every dated claim carries a source and vintage; claims are labelled **[verified]** (independently corroborated in this pass), **[reported]** (single credible source / press aggregation), or **[unverified]** (found but not corroborated). Figures state their vintage.*

---

## The argument in one paragraph

India enters the post-2024 world as, once again, the residual rice supplier: 2025 exports rebounded to **21.55 million tonnes (+19.4% y/y), the second-highest ever** [reported], after the July-2023 ban was fully unwound. But the forward opportunity is not symmetrical across markets, and the binding constraint is never demand — it is **reliability, logistics, and price**. The flagship diplomatic corridor (Afghanistan via Chabahar) is being **structurally narrowed by US sanctions**, not widened, just as India re-engages the Taliban; the landlocked west is a humanitarian line, not a commercial one, because Central Asian wheat has already displaced everyone else there. The genuinely commercial-plus-strategic core is the **neighbourhood** (Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, and the standing-quota micro-states) and **West/East Africa** — precisely the markets India's own bans burned in 2007-08 and 2022-24, so winning them back is a reliability problem India created. The Gulf is largely the **inverse** trade: Gulf sovereign capital (SALIC's $1.78 bn Olam Agri buyout; the UAE's $2 bn food-park corridor) is buying *into* Indian agriculture faster than India is selling grain *to* the Gulf. The MSV thesis's payoff here is direct: surplus grain is a real strategic instrument, but only if India stops treating export access as a discretionary on/off switch and starts signalling a **predictable exportable surplus** — the same institutional gap (NCEL's 3-4% execution, no market-development apparatus) that the companion [playbook](./agri-trader-playbook.md) diagnoses domestically.

---

## Evaluation framework

Each cluster is scored on five axes, then given a verdict:

1. **Demand & deficit** — what grain the market needs and its import volume.
2. **India's current supply/aid** — what India actually ships today.
3. **Logistics corridor & its binding constraint** — the physical channel and what throttles it.
4. **Competition** — who else supplies (Pakistan, Russia/Ukraine wheat, Thailand/Vietnam/Myanmar rice, China, Central Asia).
5. **Verdict** — one of: **Humanitarian-only** (no commercial line, aid/diplomacy value only) · **Commercial** (a real, price-competitive export market) · **Strategic** (commercial value amplified by a geopolitical return) · **Inverse** (the capital/asset flow runs *toward* India).

## 1. Afghanistan — the flagship, and a narrowing corridor

**Demand & deficit.** Afghanistan is a chronic wheat-deficit state: domestic output ~4.54 Mt against consumption needs leaving a structural gap of **2–3.3 Mt/yr** of wheat and flour, filled by imports of ~3.7 Mt/yr [reported — FEWS NET Oct 2025 / Nov 2025 outlook; [DID Press](https://en.didpress.com/21746/), 2025]. Acute food insecurity remains **IPC Phase 3 (Crisis) through at least May 2026, with Phase 4 (Emergency) pockets emerging in early 2026** in Faryab, Ghor and Daykundi [verified — [FEWS NET](https://fews.net/middle-east-and-asia/afghanistan/food-security-outlook/october-2025), Oct 2025; [IPC via ReliefWeb](https://reliefweb.int/report/afghanistan/afghanistan-ipc-acute-food-insecurity-analysis-september-2024-march-2025-published-7-january-2025), Jan 2025]. The 2026 harvest is forecast to improve access but not close the gap [reported — [FEWS NET Feb 2026 outlook](https://fews.net/asia/afghanistan/food-security-outlook/february-2026)].

**India's current supply/aid.** Purely humanitarian, and episodic:
- **2017:** first Chabahar wheat shipment, launching a **1.1 Mt aid programme** flagged off by Sushma Swaraj [reported — [Deccan Herald](https://www.deccanherald.com/india/india-starts-sending-wheat-to-af-via-irans-chabahar-port-616071.html), 2017].
- **2019–20:** **75,000 t** wheat via Chabahar (from Kandla, in 10 instalments Apr–Sep 2020) [reported — [DNA](https://www.dnaindia.com/india/report-india-to-send-75000-tonnes-of-wheat-to-afghanistan-2797305)].
- **2022–23:** **50,000 t pledge** post-Taliban takeover; ~**40,000 t via Pakistan land transit** + **20,000 t via Chabahar** to Herat; **47,500 t delivered by Aug 2023** [verified in rice-diplomacy-history.md; corroborated [Business Standard](https://www.business-standard.com/india-news/india-supplied-47-500-mt-of-wheat-to-afghanistan-since-taliban-s-takeover-123082000014_1.html)].
- **Sep 2025:** three container-loads of earthquake relief via Chabahar [reported — [NewsOnAir](https://www.newsonair.gov.in/external-affairs-ministry-india-delivers-relief-materials-to-earthquake-hit-afghanistan/), Sep 2025].

Cumulative Indian wheat aid since 2021 (~47,500 t/yr peak) is **1–1.5% of Afghanistan's ~3.7 Mt annual import need** — a humanitarian gesture, not a supply line.

**Logistics corridor & its binding constraint — the Chabahar story.** Pakistan blocks the land route (it admits Afghan, never Indian, trucks, and only intermittently), so India's durable channel is **Chabahar port (Iran) + the India-built Zaranj–Delaram road** into Afghanistan. The commitment was deepened on **13 May 2024** when India Ports Global Ltd (IPGL) signed a **10-year operating contract** with Iran's Ports & Maritime Organization, committing **~$120 m in equipment plus a $250 m rupee credit window** [verified — [Business Standard](https://www.business-standard.com/amp/economy/news/after-long-wait-india-inks-chabahar-port-s-10-year-deal-with-iran-124051301357_1.html), May 2024; [ICWA](https://www.icwa.in/show_content.php?lang=1&level=3&ls_id=10919&lid=6938)]. **But the corridor is now being throttled by US sanctions, not expanded:**
- The 2018 US sanctions waiver that ring-fenced Chabahar (originally to enable Afghan reconstruction) was **revoked effective 29 September 2025** under the Trump "maximum-pressure" policy [verified — [Tribune](https://www.tribuneindia.com/news/world/us-revokes-sanctions-waiver-for-irans-chabahar-port-effective-september-29); [Maritime Executive](https://maritime-executive.com/article/u-s-revokes-sanctions-waiver-on-iran-s-chabahar-port), 2025].
- A **6-month wind-down waiver** was then granted, running **29 October 2025 → 26 April 2026**, explicitly to let India *unwind* — not sustain — its involvement [verified — [Deccan Herald](https://www.deccanherald.com/india/us-grants-6-months-waiver-from-sanctions-on-indias-chabahar-port-project-3780337); [InsightsIAS](https://www.insightsonindia.com/2025/10/31/india-granted-us-sanctions-waiver-for-chabahar-port/), Oct 2025].
- India is reportedly weighing an **exit**, with a proposal for IPGL to sell its stake in the Chabahar free-zone entity to a local Iranian entity [reported — [WION](https://www.wionews.com/india-news/india-chabahar-port-exit-us-sanctions-waiver-1777030983122); [TBS](https://www.tbsnews.net/world/south-asia/india-weighs-future-irans-chabahar-port-us-sanctions-clock-runs-out-1421691), 2025].

Chabahar's throughput was modest even before sanctions: **~4.2 Mt of cargo handled in 2023, of which only ~15,000 t was transit cargo (0.1% of Iran's transit)** [reported — port statistics cited via [Business Standard/ICWA](https://www.icwa.in/show_content.php?lang=1&level=3&ls_id=10919&lid=6938)]. So the binding constraint is now **doubled**: US sanctions risk on the Iran leg, *and* the port never scaled to commercial grain-corridor volumes.

**Competition.** India cannot win Afghanistan commercially and does not try to. **Central Asia has already displaced Pakistan and everyone else**: Afghanistan imports **~$689 m of wheat/flour from Uzbekistan, Kazakhstan and Russia**, while **Pakistan's share has fallen effectively to zero** (from $320 m in 2015), driven by Kabul–Islamabad political friction and Central Asia's superiority on **price, quality and continuity** [reported — [DID Press](https://en.didpress.com/21746/), 2025]. These suppliers are geographically adjacent and rail-connected; Indian wheat routed via Chabahar is structurally more expensive and slower. The **Qosh-Tepa canal** could eventually make Afghanistan a net wheat *exporter* [reported — DID Press].

**Taliban-engagement constraint (easing).** India has moved from non-recognition toward active engagement: FS Vikram Misri met acting FM Muttaqi in **Dubai, Jan 2025**; **Muttaqi made a week-long visit to New Delhi, 9–15 Oct 2025** (first high-level in-person meeting; India announced it would **reopen its Kabul embassy**); Commerce Minister Noorudin Azizi led a **trade delegation 19–25 Nov 2025** [verified — [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/10/14/afghan-foreign-minister-in-india-why-new-delhi-is-embracing-taliban-now); [ICWA](https://www.icwa.in/show_content.php?lang=1&level=3&ls_id=13757&lid=8365), 2025]. The diplomatic door is opening precisely as the logistics door (Chabahar) is being pushed shut by Washington.

> **Verdict: Humanitarian-only, and structurally so.** Afghanistan is the emotional flagship of India's grain diplomacy but the weakest commercial case. Central Asia owns the market on price/proximity; the one Indian corridor (Chabahar) is a sanctions liability through at least April 2026; and India's own volumes are ~1% of need. The MSV value here is pure soft power — keep the humanitarian line open as a diplomatic asset (it buys the "not allow territory to be used against India" assurance), but do not model Afghanistan as an export market. **If Chabahar goes, the corridor argument for the entire landlocked west weakens with it.**

## 2. Central Asia via the INSTC — a transit and pulses play, not a grain-deficit market

**Demand & deficit.** The task brief's premise holds: **Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan are wheat *exporters*, not importers** (Kazakhstan is Afghanistan's main supplier, §1). So there is no grain-deficit to sell into. The realistic Indian offering is **pulses, rice and processed food** into a region that grows little of them, plus using the corridor for **re-export/transit** toward Russia and Europe.

**India's current supply / corridor status.** The **International North–South Transport Corridor (INSTC)** — India→Iran (Bandar Abbas/Chabahar)→Caspian→Russia/Central Asia — carried **26.9 Mt of freight in 2024 (+19% y/y)**, with rail >12.9 Mt, targeting 15 Mt/yr of a specific rail segment by 2027 [reported — ORF/ADB summaries, 2024–25]. Since **March 2025** India has shipped cargo Mundra→Bandar Abbas→Central Asia on this route, and a Nov-2025 test train ran Moscow→Tehran in 12 days [reported — [ORF](https://www.orfonline.org/expert-speak/instc-eastern-corridor-india-s-gateway-to-central-asia); GlobalSecurity, 2025]. **Caveat:** the INSTC's Iran leg shares Chabahar's sanctions exposure, and the corridor is still transit-cost-competitive mainly versus Suez, not versus Central Asia's own overland grain flows.

**Competition & realism.** For pulses specifically, India is a *buyer* from this direction (Russian yellow peas, §4), not a seller. The corridor's grain relevance is therefore: (a) a **cheaper import channel** for pulses/oilseeds India is short of, and (b) a **transit rail** that could carry Indian rice/processed food to Russian and Central Asian retail — but at volumes that are rounding errors against India's 21 Mt rice book.

> **Verdict: Strategic (connectivity), not a grain-export market.** INSTC's value to the MSV thesis is as an *inbound* pulses-security channel and a geopolitical Suez-hedge, not an outlet for the rice/wheat mountain. Treat Central Asia as a niche processed-food and re-export destination; do not count it toward exportable-surplus monetisation. Its fate is also tied to Chabahar/Iran sanctions (§1).

## 3. The neighbourhood — the realistic commercial + strategic core

This is where surplus grain is genuinely both saleable and strategic, because these markets are structurally import-dependent, geographically captive (short hauls, land/short-sea), and politically sensitive to Indian supply.

**Bangladesh.** The largest single opportunity — and the clearest reliability cautionary tale. A major rice *and* wheat importer, Bangladesh buys Indian non-basmati whenever India is open (2024: rice imports from India ~$84.6 m [reported — OEC]). During its 2024–25 flood shortfall India shipped **200,000 t parboiled rice via G2G tender**; for FY2025-26 Dhaka approved another **50,000 t Indian non-basmati parboiled at $359.77/t** (supplier Bagadiya Brothers), first shipment 24,690 t landing Dec-2025 [reported — [TBS](https://www.tbsnews.net/bangladesh/govt-approves-import-50000-tonnes-rice-india-22-lakh-tonnes-wheat-us-1254856); [NewsOnAir](https://www.newsonair.gov.in/2nd-shipment-of-27000-tons-of-rice-from-india-to-arrive-in-bangladesh/), 2025]. **The reliability warning:** in the *same* procurement round Bangladesh bought **220,000 t of wheat from the United States on a G2G basis** (Agrocorp, ~$308/t) rather than from India [reported — same TBS/Daily Star, 2025]. Dhaka structurally diversifies away from India on wheat precisely because India's export switch is unpredictable — the ban-reliability cost made visible in a single tender.

**Sri Lanka.** Post-2022, India is the **top rice-import origin ($64.3 m in 2024, ahead of Pakistan $6.43 m and China $1.69 m)** [reported — [OEC](https://oec.world/en/profile/bilateral-product/rice/reporter/lka)]. Rice moved inside the 2022 ~$4 bn rescue envelope (§2.3 of the history: ~16,000 t under the $1 bn SBI credit line + 11,000 t goodwill). As Sri Lanka's paddy recovers (milled-rice output forecast **3.45 Mt for MY2026-27**, imports easing to **~100,000 t**) the *emergency* rice-diplomacy window is closing, but India retains pole position as the default supplier of last resort [reported — [USDA via Daily Mirror](https://www.dailymirror.lk/business-main/Sri-Lanka%E2%80%99s-rice-production-poised-for-recovery-amid-lingering-climate-and-global-supply-hurdles---USDA/245-339357), 2025].

**Nepal, Bhutan, Maldives — the standing-exemption bloc.** These are the purest strategic-lock markets: all three hold **assured-supply carve-outs that survive India's export bans** (Bhutan's open-ended exemption; Maldives' treaty-like annual quotas raised 5% for FY25 and maintained *through* the 2024 diplomatic chill; Nepal's 95,000 t was the single largest Asian rice quota of the 2023 round) [verified in rice-diplomacy-history.md §2.4/§4.2]. The commercial volumes are small; the strategic value (neighbourhood lock-in, denying the space to China/Pakistan) is high and cheap.

**Myanmar.** A rice *competitor/exporter* (it supplied the markets India vacated in 2022-24), not an Indian grain market — but a two-way pulses counterparty (a major black-matpe/pigeon-pea source for India). Treat as competition + inbound pulses, not an outlet.

> **Verdict: Commercial + Strategic — the core.** Bangladesh and Sri Lanka are real, price-sensitive, repeat commercial buyers where India has geographic and logistical advantage (short hauls, established G2G machinery). The micro-states are low-volume, high-strategic locks. **The single biggest lever here is not price — it is a credible no-ban commitment.** Every US-wheat-to-Bangladesh tender is a measurable cost of India's reliability deficit. This is the cluster where the MSV "predictable exportable-surplus signalling" idea (§7) pays off most directly.

## 4. Africa — the biggest non-basmati market, and the reliability-cost laboratory

**Demand & deficit.** West Africa (Benin, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Guinea, Gambia) is the world's largest buyer of Indian **parboiled and broken rice**; East Africa (Kenya, Tanzania, Mozambique) is both a rice market *and* India's **pulses source** — a genuinely two-way relationship. Pre-2022, India shipped **1–2 Mt of broken rice annually** to this belt [reported — [Rice News Today](https://ricenewstoday.com/resumption-of-broken-rice-exports-to-boost-growth-in-west-africa-indonesia-markets/), 2025].

**The reliability cost, quantified.** India's bans devastated this market and the numbers are stark. Broken-rice export value collapsed **$1.13 bn (FY22) → $983 m (FY23) → $194 m (FY24)** — an **~83% fall** [reported — Rice News Today, 2025]. Total Indian rice exports to Africa fell **5.3 Mt (−43%) in Sep 2022–Aug 2023**, ceding share to **Vietnam, Thailand, Pakistan and Myanmar**, whose quotes hit ~15-year highs [verified in rice-diplomacy-history.md §synthesis, USDA FAS/IFPRI]. The 2007-08 precedent shows the memory is long: the same West African states hit twice in fifteen years now diversify structurally.

**The 2025 recovery.** With the broken-rice ban lifted **March 2025** and all rice curbs gone, India's **2025 total rice exports rebounded to 21.55 Mt (+19.4%), the second-highest on record**, pushing Asian prices to near-decade lows and explicitly re-winning **Benin, Cameroon, Côte d'Ivoire and Djibouti** alongside Bangladesh [reported — [Reuters via MarketScreener](https://www.marketscreener.com/news/india-s-2025-rice-exports-surge-to-near-record-as-curbs-lifted-ce7e59d3dc8ff321); [Eminent IAS](https://www.eminent-ias.com/daily-updates/india-s-rice-exports-rebound-to-near-record-high-after-removal-of-export-restrictions), 2025]. APEDA resumed export registration for Philippines, Senegal etc. [reported — [Rice News Today](https://ricenewstoday.com/apeda-resumes-rice-export-registration-to-the-philippines-senegal-after-import-curbs-lifted/)]. **Can India win share back?** On *price*, yes — India's cost structure and record stocks (36.9 Mt rice, ~2.7× the 13.58 Mt buffer norm) let it undercut and it is doing so. On *trust*, only partially — buyers now keep Vietnamese/Pakistani/Thai relationships warm as insurance, which permanently caps India's peak share below the pre-2022 level.

**The two-way pulses dimension (East Africa).** India runs **tur/pigeon-pea import MoUs with Mozambique (200,000 t to 2025-26) and Tanzania**; in 2024-25 it imported **1.2 Mt of tur** from Mozambique, Tanzania, Myanmar, Sudan and Malawi [reported — [Drishti IAS](https://www.drishtiias.com/daily-updates/daily-news-analysis/india-to-import-tur-dal-from-mozambique); [Deccan Herald](https://www.deccanherald.com/amp/story/business%2Ftanzania-seeks-removal-of-trade-curbs-on-green-gram-extension-of-pulse-import-pact-with-india-3404311), 2025]. This gives India a **barter-like strategic hand** in East Africa — sell rice, buy pulses — that West Africa lacks, and a structural reason to keep the relationship reliable in *both* directions (India needs the pulses).

> **Verdict: Commercial (West Africa) + Strategic two-way (East Africa).** This is the largest pure-commercial outlet for the non-basmati surplus and the clearest place the MSV reliability thesis is tested. India can win on price and is recovering fast in 2025, but the ceiling is now lower because bans taught buyers to diversify. East Africa is strategically richer because pulses flow back — a reason to make it a no-ban priority. Africa is exactly the market a **USGC-style market-development office** (offices in Dakar/Nairobi/Cotonou; par-boiled-rice quality and fumigation servicing) would defend against the Vietnam/Thai/Pakistan re-entry.

## 5. The Gulf — mostly the *inverse* trade

**India as food supplier to the Gulf.** Real but bounded. India exported **~$503.7 m of cereals to the UAE in 2024, of which rice was $487.88 m** [reported — Trading Economics/COMTRADE]; UAE–India non-oil trade grew **+34% to $38 bn in H1 2025** on CEPA utilisation, with agri/processed food among the fastest-expanding sectors [reported — [Middle East Briefing](https://www.middleeastbriefing.com/news/india-uae-cepa-non-oil-trade-and-transshipment-of-goods/), 2025]. The **I2U2 India–UAE "food corridor"** envisages **$2 bn of UAE investment in integrated food parks across India** (India provides land + farmer integration; US/Israel bring agri-tech) [verified — [Business Standard](https://www.business-standard.com/industry/news/india-uae-targets-food-corridor-with-2-billion-initial-investment-124100700975_1.html), Oct 2024; [State Dept I2U2](https://www.state.gov/i2u2)]. **Critical nuance:** India **excluded cereals from CEPA tariff concessions to protect domestic producers** — so the corridor is about *processed food and re-export via Jebel Ali*, not liberalised bulk-grain access [reported — Middle East Briefing, 2025].

**The reverse flow — Gulf capital buying into Indian agri.** This is the bigger story. On **24 Feb 2025** Saudi Arabia's **SALIC (PIF-owned) agreed to raise its Olam Agri stake from 35.4% to 80.01% for $1.78 bn**, with a call option on the rest — taking control of one of the world's largest grain/rice traders (30+ countries) explicitly for **Saudi food security under Vision 2030** [verified — [PIF/SALIC](https://www.salic.com/news/Olam); [Arab News](https://www.arabnews.com/node/2641453/business-economy), 2025]. Olam Agri is a *major mover of Indian rice*, so a Gulf sovereign now sits astride part of India's own export logistics. The direction of strategic capital is **Gulf→India**, not grain India→Gulf.

> **Verdict: Inverse / Strategic-capital, not a grain-export growth market.** The Gulf's food-security anxiety is met less by buying Indian grain (cereals are CEPA-protected) than by buying Indian *farmland-processing capacity* (I2U2 food parks) and *global trading assets that carry Indian grain* (SALIC-Olam). For the MSV thesis this reframes the Gulf as a **source of patient capital and guaranteed offtake infrastructure** — the food parks are a domestic MSV-adjacent asset, not an export line. India should negotiate the food-park corridor as a way to *contract offtake in advance* (the playbook's core prescription), with Gulf capital de-risking storage and processing.

## 6. Others where ties are deepening

**Egypt.** The 2022 test case of India-as-Black-Sea-substitute — and a logistics/quality humbling. Egypt approved India as a wheat origin (Apr 2022) and India targeted **3 Mt for MY2022-23**; the wheat-export ban's G2G carve-out let a partly loaded cargo sail. **Actual delivery fell to ~1 Mt, blocked by quality and logistics/fumigation issues** [reported — [S&P Global](https://www.spglobal.com/commodityinsights/en/market-insights/latest-news/agriculture/042022-india-may-export-1-mil-mt-wheat-to-egypt-in-my-2022-23-fall-short-of-target-sources); Business Standard, 2022]. The episode is a warning that Indian wheat is not yet spec-competitive for demanding importers, and that India's wheat surplus is thinner/less exportable than its rice surplus. Egypt later received a 60,000 t rice quota in the 2023-24 round.

**Indonesia & the Philippines.** Standby options, largely unexercised. Indonesia's 2023 **1 Mt MoU** was mostly a reassurance instrument; by 2025 Indonesia had swung to **self-sufficiency (3.52 Mt surplus, no consumer-rice imports in 2025, eyeing exports in 2026)** — so this market has *closed* for now [reported — [VOI](https://voi.id/en/opini/550382), 2025]. The Philippines is a live, price-driven buyer: India exported **40,447 t of rice ($49.29 m) to the Philippines in Apr–Oct 2025-26** with APEDA registration resumed [reported — Trading Economics; Rice News Today, 2025], but Manila buys overwhelmingly from Vietnam and Thailand on proximity — India is a marginal supplier, not the default.

**Vietnam.** A competitor, not a market — it re-took African/Asian share during India's ban and remains the price benchmark India must beat. No Indian grain export case exists.

> **Verdict:** Egypt = **occasional G2G/humanitarian** (wheat spec-limited); Indonesia = **dormant** (self-sufficient); Philippines = **thin commercial** (price-taker behind Vietnam/Thailand); Vietnam = **competition**.

## 7. Synthesis for the MSV project

The MSV thesis holds that MSP-minted surplus is a *strategic asset* whose value pure carry-cost accounting misses. This evaluation adds the forward, market-by-market test — and four binding constraints determine which markets are real.

**(a) Reliability is the binding constraint, and India's bans are the single biggest barrier.** Every cluster returns the same verdict: demand is never the problem; *trust* is. Bangladesh buys US wheat in the same tender it buys Indian rice (§3). West Africa's Indian broken-rice value fell 83% and buyers structurally diversified (§4). Egypt hedges to Russia/Black Sea (§6). The 2025 rebound to 21.55 Mt proves India can *win on price the moment it opens* — but also that its peak share is now capped by the insurance relationships buyers built during the bans. **Reliability is the asset; each ban amortises it.** An MSV-era export strategy's first move is not a new market — it is a **credible, rules-based no-ban commitment** so that "assured supply" means the same thing to Dhaka and Dakar that it already means to Thimphu and Malé.

**(b) Logistics splits the map cleanly.** The **landlocked west (Afghanistan, Central Asia)** depends on **Chabahar/INSTC**, both now under US-sanctions cloud through at least April 2026 — so that entire flank reverts to humanitarian-only regardless of intent (§1–2). The **maritime markets (neighbourhood, Africa, Gulf)** depend on **port and fumigation capacity**, where the constraint is quality-spec and throughput, not geopolitics. Capital should follow the map: **do not build an export thesis on Chabahar**; build it on Kolkata/Kakinada/Vizag→Africa and the short-sea neighbourhood.

**(c) The NCEL execution gap remains the institutional bottleneck.** NCEL's 2023-24 record (3.9% of allocated non-basmati rice actually shipped by April 2024) captured diplomatic credit without delivering grain. By Aug 2025 NCEL reports **13.49 LMT / ₹5,403 cr across 28 countries and 61 importer MoUs** [reported — [Free Press Journal](https://www.freepressjournal.in/amp/business/national-co-operative-exports-achieves-540301-crore-milestone-of-exporting-farm-goods); PIB, Sep 2025] — improving, but still a nominated cooperative substituting for trading capability. The playbook's remedy stands: **auction tradeable export quotas** rather than nominate a monopoly — the state captures the scarcity rent transparently, professional traders (Olam — now Saudi-owned, LDC, ITC, Indian houses) execute the logistics they are demonstrably better at, and diplomacy still rides on top via country-reserved tranches.

**(d) Price competition is winnable but narrowing.** India's cost structure and 36.9 Mt rice stocks let it undercut Vietnam/Thailand/Pakistan (the 2025 rebound proves it), and on parboiled/broken rice India is the natural low-cost supplier to Africa. But on **wheat** India is *not* spec-competitive (Egypt's 3→1 Mt shortfall), and Central Asia beats it on proximity for Afghanistan. **The exportable surplus is a rice story, not a wheat story.**

### The market verdict matrix

| Cluster | Demand | India's edge | Binding constraint | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Afghanistan** | 3.3 Mt wheat gap | Diplomacy only | Chabahar sanctions + Central Asia owns price | **Humanitarian-only** |
| **Central Asia / INSTC** | None (exporters) | Transit + inbound pulses | Iran-leg sanctions | **Strategic (connectivity), not export** |
| **Bangladesh** | Large rice+wheat | Proximity, G2G machinery | India's own reliability | **Commercial + Strategic (core)** |
| **Sri Lanka** | Recovering, ~100kt | Default last-resort supplier | Its own recovery shrinks window | **Commercial + Strategic** |
| **Nepal/Bhutan/Maldives** | Small | Standing ban-exemptions | Low volume | **Strategic lock (cheap)** |
| **West Africa** | 1-2 Mt broken/parboiled | Low-cost, recovering | Post-ban trust ceiling | **Commercial (largest)** |
| **East Africa** | Rice + pulses two-way | Barter-like pulses return | Trust; needs reliability both ways | **Strategic two-way** |
| **Gulf (UAE/Saudi)** | Food-security anxiety | Cereals CEPA-excluded | — | **Inverse (capital→India)** |
| **Egypt** | World's top wheat buyer | Occasional G2G | Wheat spec/quality | **Occasional / humanitarian** |

| **Philippines** | Large | Price-taker behind Vietnam | Proximity to SEA | **Thin commercial** |
| **Vietnam** | — | — | — | **Competition** |

### What an MSV-era export strategy would do differently

1. **Signal a predictable exportable surplus.** Publish, each season, an **exportable-surplus band** (stocks above buffer norm earmarked for export) so buyers can plan — the opposite of the overnight-ban switch. This is the single highest-return reform: it directly attacks constraint (a) and costs nothing.
2. **Auction the export right, don't nominate it.** Replace NCEL's discretionary G2G monopoly with **quota auctions** (with country-reserved diplomatic tranches carved out first), capturing the scarcity rent transparently and handing execution to professional traders — fixing constraint (c).
3. **Stand up a USGC-style market-development office.** India has *no* permanent overseas grain-market office; the US Grains Council funds one *in New Delhi* to open India's market (playbook §5). The mirror institution would run **parboiled-rice quality/fumigation servicing in Dakar/Nairobi/Cotonou, feed-trial and de-oiled-rice-bran programmes in Dhaka/Hanoi/Jakarta, and a standing barrier-removal docket** — defending the African and neighbourhood share the 2025 rebound is winning back before Vietnam/Thailand re-entrench.
4. **Treat Gulf capital as contracted offtake, not a grain market.** Negotiate the I2U2 food parks and SALIC-type capital as **advance-offtake and storage/processing de-risking** for the domestic MSV circuit — the playbook's "contract the offtake before the purchase" prescription, funded by patient sovereign capital.
5. **Concede Afghanistan and the west as humanitarian.** Keep the aid line (it buys real strategic assurances) but stop treating Chabahar as a commercial grain corridor — its sanctions exposure and Central Asia's price dominance make that a dead end through at least 2026.

**The through-line to the MSV thesis:** surplus grain *is* a strategic instrument, but its Minimum Strategic Value is realised only where India can be a **trusted, priced, rules-based supplier** — the neighbourhood and Africa — and it is *destroyed* wherever India uses the export switch as a blunt domestic-price tool. The same institutional discipline the MSV project prescribes at home (capped, pre-announced volumes; contracted offtake; transparent pricing of hidden subsidies) is exactly what converts these markets from episodic diplomacy into durable strategic-commercial assets.
